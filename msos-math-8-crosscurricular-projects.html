🏛️ SOCIAL STUDIES

Manhattan Project — Half-Life of Fallout

Math 8 + Social Studies 8, Unit 3 (World War II)
⏱ 2 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Exponential decay (N = N₀ × (1/2)^(t/T))
  • Half-life formula
  • Scientific notation
  • Logarithmic interpretation (optional)

🏛️ Social Studies Content

  • WWII Manhattan Project history
  • Hiroshima & Nagasaki
  • Ethics of weapons science
  • Long-term consequences of historical events

📋 Materials

  • Calculator (scientific)
  • Graph paper or spreadsheet
  • Reference: Cs-137 half-life 30.17 yr; Sr-90 half-life 28.79 yr; I-131 half-life 8.02 days

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Research the bombs. Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945, ~64 kg uranium, 16 kt TNT yield). Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945, ~6 kg plutonium, 21 kt yield).
  2. Identify key isotopes in fallout. Cesium-137, Strontium-90, Iodine-131. Look up half-lives.
  3. Apply the decay formula. N = N₀ × (1/2)^(t/T). Compute fraction remaining at t = 10, 30, 80, 200 years for each isotope.
  4. Express results in scientific notation. At t = 80 years (2025): Cs-137 fraction = (1/2)^(80/30.17) ≈ (1/2)^2.65 ≈ 0.16 or 1.6 × 10⁻¹.
  5. Graph the decay curves. One curve per isotope, on the same axes. Mark today (2025).
  6. Compute "safe" threshold. Typically < 1% of original is considered low-risk. How many half-lives is that? When did each isotope reach that threshold?
  7. Connect to history. Hiroshima Peace Memorial: what does the math reveal about lasting consequences? Why did this lead to the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty?
  8. Write the report. 3 pages with graphs, calculations, and a 1-page ethical reflection on the responsibility of scientists who unlock such power.

📦 Deliverable

3-page report with decay graphs + 1-page ethical-historical reflection.

📖 ENGLISH LANGUAGE ARTS

The Pearl's Worth — A Linear Negotiation Model

Math 8 + ELA 8, Unit 3 (The Pearl by Steinbeck)
⏱ 1.5 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Linear functions (y = mx + b)
  • Slope & intercept
  • Percent error
  • Data interpretation

📖 English Language Arts Content

  • Steinbeck's novella The Pearl
  • Themes: greed, value, fate, family
  • Connecting math to moral arc of a story

📋 Materials

  • A copy of The Pearl
  • Graph paper or spreadsheet
  • Calculator

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Track every offer. Re-read the chapters where Kino negotiates the pearl. Record each appraisal value and the day it was offered.
  2. Plot offers vs. day. Create a scatter plot on graph paper or spreadsheet.
  3. Identify the trend. Do offers go up, down, or stay flat? Why does Steinbeck arrange it this way?
  4. Fit a linear function. y = mx + b, where y = pearl value and x = days from finding. Compute slope and y-intercept from the data.
  5. Compute percent error. What's the "fair value" (e.g., 1,500 pesos per Kino's research)? Each appraisal's percent error from that fair value.
  6. Identify the cheaters. Which buyer's offer had the highest percent error (most under-valued)?
  7. Connect to the moral arc. The pearl's monetary devaluation parallels Kino's spiritual unraveling. How does the math mirror the theme?
  8. Write the analysis. 2-page essay with citations from the novel, the linear equation you found, and one quote that captures the moment Kino realizes the math is rigged against him.

📦 Deliverable

Scatter plot with linear regression + percent-error table + 2-page literary-mathematical analysis.

✝️ RELIGION

Gothic Cathedrals — Arch, Slope & Stress

Math 8 + Religion 8, Unit 3 (Age of Faith and Beauty)
⏱ 2 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Pythagorean theorem
  • Slope and rate of change
  • Linear functions (stress distribution)
  • Geometry of arches

✝️ Religion Content

  • Medieval Christianity & cathedral building
  • Gothic vs. Romanesque architecture
  • Sacred geometry as a form of prayer

📋 Materials

  • Photos of Notre Dame, Chartres, Sainte-Chapelle (online)
  • Cardstock or thick paper for arch model
  • Ruler
  • Protractor
  • Calculator

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Compare arches. Sketch a Romanesque (round) arch and a Gothic (pointed) arch on graph paper, same width. The pointed arch is taller for the same span.
  2. Apply Pythagorean. For the Gothic arch, the side stones make a triangle. If span is 4 m and rise is 5 m, what's the slant stone length? Use Pythagorean: √(2² + 5²) ≈ 5.39 m.
  3. Compute slope of a flying buttress. A buttress typically rises 8 m over a horizontal span of 4 m. Slope = rise/run = 2.
  4. Model stress as a linear function. Horizontal thrust on the buttress = (weight of roof × height of arch) / span. Plot stress vs. arch height.
  5. Predict. If you doubled the arch height, what happens to the thrust? Doubles. Why does this matter structurally?
  6. Build a Gothic arch model. Cut cardstock into 6 wedge stones following your geometry; assemble without glue (the wedges hold themselves up).
  7. Spiritual reflection. Gothic architects believed pointed arches symbolized hands praying. The math allowed taller, lighter, more luminous churches with stained glass. How does the math serve the prayer?
  8. Final piece. 2-page essay with cathedral photo references, your math, and the model.

📦 Deliverable

Cardstock Gothic-arch model + 1-page math worksheet (arches + buttresses) + 2-page architectural-spiritual essay.

🌏 FOREIGN LANGUAGES

Lunar Calendar Mathematics — Three Calendars Compared

Math 8 + Foreign Languages 8 (Hawaiian & Mandarin cultural calendars)
⏱ 1.5 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Modular arithmetic
  • Ratios (lunar month ≈ 29.5 days vs. Gregorian 30.44)
  • Calendar conversion algorithms
  • Linear drift of lunar vs. solar

🌏 Foreign Languages Content

  • Hawaiian Kaulana Mahina (moon calendar with 30 named nights)
  • Chinese lunar calendar & zodiac
  • Cultural significance of time-keeping

📋 Materials

  • Reference: Hawaiian moon-night names (Hilo, Hoaka, Kū-Kahi…), Chinese months & zodiac, Gregorian calendar
  • Calculator

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Learn the 30 Hawaiian moon-night names. Each represents a specific phase of the moon & traditionally informs fishing, planting, prayer.
  2. Learn the 12 Chinese lunar months & zodiac. Identify your birth year's zodiac.
  3. Map one Hawaiian moon cycle (29.5 days) onto a Gregorian month (30 or 31 days). What's the offset by the end of the year?
  4. Compute the drift. 12 Hawaiian lunar months × 29.5 days = 354 days. Gregorian year = 365.25 days. Drift = 11.25 days/year.
  5. Find Chinese New Year for 3 years. 2026, 2027, 2028. Look up the dates. Notice the drift relative to Gregorian.
  6. Apply modular arithmetic. If today's Gregorian date is day X of the lunar month, what's tomorrow? Day (X+1) mod 29.5.
  7. Build a 3-calendar conversion chart. For one month, list each day in Gregorian, the Hawaiian moon-night name, and the Chinese lunar day.
  8. Reflect. 1 page: how do different cultures' time systems shape worldview? Why might a fishing culture (Hawai‘i) use a moon calendar?

📦 Deliverable

3-calendar conversion chart for one month + 1-page cultural-mathematical reflection.
