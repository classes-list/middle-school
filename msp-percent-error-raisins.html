🍇 Percent Error: Counting Raisins

Estimate first, count second, and see how close you got.

🎯 The Big Question

Every measurement has some error — even careful ones. Percent error tells you how close your measurement came to the true value.

Essential Question: How accurate was our estimate of the number of raisins in the box?

📖 Vocabulary

TermWhat it isHow you get it
Experimental ValueWhat YOU measured or estimated.Estimate the # of raisins in the box (take 10 out to get an idea of size).
Accepted ValueThe theoretical or true value.Count how many raisins are actually in the box.
ErrorHow far your value was from the true one.| experimental − accepted |
Percent ErrorHow accurate your data was, as a percentage.(error ÷ accepted) × 100

🧮 The Formulas

error = | experimental − accepted |
% error = (error ÷ accepted) × 100

Notice that error is always positive (that's what the absolute-value bars mean). You're just measuring the distance from the true value, not the direction.

🍇 The Raisin Lab

Materials (per group)

  • One small box of raisins
  • Data sheet + pencil
  • Optional: a small paper plate for counting

Procedure

Pour 10 raisins out onto the plate. Look at how much space they take up.
Look at the box. Without pouring anything else out yet, estimate how many raisins are in the whole box. This is your experimental value.
Now count every single raisin in the box. This is your accepted value.
Calculate error using the formula above.
Calculate percent error.
Compare with other groups. Whose estimate was closest?

💭 Worked Example

You estimated 60 raisins. You counted 72 raisins.

  • error = | 60 − 72 | = 12 raisins
  • % error = (12 ÷ 72) × 100 = 16.7%

💬 Discussion

  1. Was your percent error less than 10%, 25%, or more? What does that tell you about your estimating skills?
  2. Would percent error be more useful, or less useful, if the accepted value were very small? (Try it: what happens to % error when accepted = 2?)
  3. Why do scientists use percent error instead of just plain error?
  4. Where else in real life do you see percent error — election polls, weather forecasts, sports predictions?
