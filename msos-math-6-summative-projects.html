Two project-based summative assessments per quarter — each designed as a 1.5- to 2.5-week capstone tied to the math standards of the quarter and (where possible) directly aligned with a STEM science assessment. Each project lists the math content, materials, step-by-step instructions, and final deliverable. MYP criteria targeted: A Knowing & Understanding, B Inquiring & Designing, C Processing & Evaluating, D Reflecting on Impacts.
Q1

Lab Math & Density

Hawaiian Rock Density Lab

⏱ 2 weeks (1 week field/lab, 1 week notebook) MYP A · B · C · D

Math Content

  • Mass & volume measurement
  • Decimal division (D = m/V)
  • KHDUdcm unit conversion (g/cm³ ↔ kg/m³)
  • Percent error calculation
  • Significant figures

Materials

  • 4 different rocks (collected outside)
  • Triple-beam balance or digital scale (0.1 g)
  • Graduated cylinder (100 mL)
  • Water for displacement
  • Composition notebook or template
  • Calculator
  • Rock-density reference table

Step-by-Step

  1. Collect. Find 4 rocks of varying composition. Each must fit in a graduated cylinder. Label them A, B, C, D and record where you found each one.
  2. Measure mass. Weigh each rock on the balance to the nearest 0.1 g. Record in your field notebook with units.
  3. Measure volume. Fill the cylinder to 50.0 mL. Slip rock A in carefully. Record the new water level. Volume = (new level − 50.0) mL.
  4. Calculate density. Compute D = m / V for each rock in g/cm³ (1 mL = 1 cm³). Use 3 significant figures.
  5. Convert units. Convert each density value to kg/m³ using the KHDUdcm staircase. Show the conversion arithmetic.
  6. Identify rock type. Match your computed density against the reference table (basalt ≈ 3.0; granite ≈ 2.7; limestone ≈ 2.5; coral ≈ 2.5; pumice < 1.0). Justify each ID.
  7. Percent error. Compute |measured − accepted| / accepted × 100% for each rock. Comment on whether your error is acceptable (under 10% = good).
  8. Compile notebook. Each rock gets a page with: photo, location, measurements, calculations, identification, percent error, and a one-sentence reflection on probable error source.

Deliverable

Field notebook (10–12 pages) with photos, measurements, density calculations, conversion work, identifications, and a 1-paragraph reflection on sources of measurement error.

Lūʻau for 50 — Recipe Scaling Project

⏱ 1.5 weeks MYP A · C · D

Math Content

  • Fraction division and multiplication
  • Greatest common factor (GCF) for bulk purchasing
  • Decimal cost-per-person
  • Unit conversion (cups → oz; tsp → tbsp)

Materials

  • A traditional Hawaiian recipe at 8 servings (kalua pig, lomi salmon, haupia, poi)
  • Current grocery flyer (Foodland or Times)
  • Calculator
  • Measuring conversion chart

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose a recipe. Pick a traditional 8-serving Hawaiian dish. Find a credible recipe with all ingredient quantities listed.
  2. Compute the scale factor. 50 ÷ 8 = 6.25. Every ingredient gets multiplied by 6.25.
  3. Scale each ingredient. Apply 6.25× to every quantity. For fractions, multiply (e.g., 3/4 cup × 6.25 = 4.6875 cups). Round sensibly for cooking.
  4. Convert units. Where the scaled amount becomes awkward (e.g., 18 tsp), convert to a larger unit (18 tsp = 6 tbsp = 3/8 cup).
  5. GCF for grouping. If you need 9.4 lb of pork and the store sells 2-lb packages, use GCF to figure out the smallest number of packages that exceeds your need (10 lb total = 5 packs).
  6. Price each ingredient. Multiply quantity needed × unit price from the flyer. Total the recipe.
  7. Cost-per-person. Total cost ÷ 50.
  8. Submit a cookbook page. Lay out the scaled recipe like a cookbook entry: ingredient list, instructions, photo (or sketch), cost-per-serving, scale factor used.

Deliverable

1-page cookbook layout + 1-page itemized shopping list with prices and total cost.

Q2

Numbers at Universe Scale

The Great Pluto Debate

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · D

Math Content

  • Ratios & scale factor
  • Scientific notation (large numbers)
  • Coordinate plane
  • Powers of 10

Materials

  • Long hallway (at least 30 m)
  • Measuring tape
  • Paper / cardstock for planet markers
  • Markers, ruler
  • Reference data for planet sizes & distances

Step-by-Step

  1. Research. Look up planet diameters (km) and distances from Sun (km). Express each in scientific notation.
  2. Choose a distance scale. Decide: 1 m of hallway = ? km of real space. Try 1 m = 30 million km (3 × 10⁷ km). This puts Neptune ~150 m away — too far. Adjust until Neptune fits the hallway.
  3. Calculate scaled distances. For each planet: scaled_distance (m) = real_distance (km) ÷ scale_factor (km/m). Show your work.
  4. Realize the diameter problem. Apply the same scale factor to diameters. Earth becomes a sub-millimeter speck! Note this in your report — sizes and distances cannot share the same scale.
  5. Pick a separate diameter scale so the planets are visible (e.g., 1 cm = 10,000 km).
  6. Build planet markers. Cut circles to the diameter-scale size, label with name, real diameter, real distance.
  7. Install in hallway. Measure scaled distances, tape markers to wall.
  8. Add a coordinate-plane map. On a separate sheet, plot each planet as an ordered pair (distance from Sun, 0). Include Pluto with a debate panel: should we count it as the 9th marker?

Deliverable

Physical hallway installation + a printed scale-map handout for visitors + a 1-page Pluto-debate position paper.

Hawai‘i Energy Mix Infographic

⏱ 1.5 weeks MYP A · C · D

Math Content

  • Percents from raw data
  • Rates (kWh per source per year)
  • Percent change vs. baseline year
  • Pie/bar chart construction

Materials

  • Hawaiian Electric energy data (publicly available)
  • Spreadsheet or Canva for infographic
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Pull data. Find Hawaiian Electric's most recent annual energy generation by source (solar, wind, biomass, oil, coal, geothermal) in kWh.
  2. Compute percentages. Each source ÷ total × 100%. Round to 1 decimal place. Verify percents sum to 100%.
  3. Find 2010 baseline data. Compute the same percentages for 2010.
  4. Calculate percent change. For each source: (new % − 2010 %) ÷ 2010 % × 100%.
  5. Project 2035 mix. Hawai‘i has a 100% renewable mandate for 2045. Sketch what the mix needs to look like by 2035 to be on pace.
  6. Per-person rate. Total kWh ÷ Hawai‘i population = annual kWh per resident.
  7. Design infographic. Two charts (pie for current mix, bar for percent change) + 4 callout statistics + headline.
  8. Print at 11×17". Ready for display.

Deliverable

Printed 11×17" infographic + 1-page methodology note (data sources and calculation steps).

Q3

Reading the Earth

Ages of the Earth Lab & Presentation

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Half-life equation: N = N₀(1/2)^(t/T)
  • Powers of 10
  • Fractions / exponents
  • Logarithms (optional — graph reading instead)

Materials

  • M&M's or pennies (about 200 per group)
  • Provided fictional rock-sample data
  • Graph paper
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Simulate decay with candy. Start with 100 M&M's facing up. Each "turn" (= 1 half-life), shake and remove every one facing down. Record how many remain after each turn for 8 turns.
  2. Graph the decay. Plot "number remaining" vs. "turn number." Confirm it halves each turn.
  3. Connect to the formula. Verify your data matches N = 100 × (1/2)^t.
  4. Pull fictional rock data. Each sample lists the percent of parent isotope remaining. (Example: Sample 1 has 25% remaining → 2 half-lives have passed.)
  5. Solve for age. Use the formula with known half-life T. For carbon-14, T = 5,730 years. For K-Ar, T = 1.25 × 10⁹ years.
  6. Date 5 Hawaiian rock samples. Use K-Ar half-life (suitable for old rocks). Report ages in scientific notation.
  7. Compare to known ages. Kīlauea lava < 1,000 years; Mauna Kea ~1 × 10⁶ years; Kaua‘i ~5 × 10⁶ years.
  8. Lab report. Submit candy simulation graph, formula derivation, 5 rock-sample calculations with ages, and comparison to known values.

Deliverable

5–7 page lab report with simulation data, decay graph, 5 dated samples, and reflection.

Trail Slope Field Guide

⏱ 2 weeks MYP B · C · D

Math Content

  • Slope = rise ÷ run
  • Topographic map reading (contour intervals)
  • Linear equations (y = mx + b)
  • Rate conversion

Materials

  • Topographic map of chosen Hawaiian trail (download from USGS)
  • Ruler
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose a Hawaiian trail. Diamond Head, Koko Crater Railway, Manoa Falls, or Lanikai Pillbox.
  2. Download the topographic map. Find the contour interval (usually 10 m or 40 ft).
  3. Identify 5 trail segments. Mark them along the trail. Each segment should cross at least one contour line.
  4. Measure horizontal distance. Use the map's scale bar to find ground distance for each segment in meters.
  5. Measure elevation gain. Count contour lines crossed × contour interval = rise in meters.
  6. Calculate slope. For each segment: slope = rise ÷ run. Express as a fraction, decimal, and percent grade.
  7. Write a linear equation. For each segment: elevation = slope × distance + starting elevation.
  8. Build the hiker's guide. One page per segment with: photo or sketch, the linear equation, percent grade, predicted minutes (assume 1 km of flat = 10 min, then add 1 min per 10 m of climb).

Deliverable

4–6 page printed trail guide with maps, equations, and pacing predictions.

Q4

Climate Data & Geometry

Hawai‘i Climate & Energy Project

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · B · C · D

Math Content

  • Mean, median, range, IQR
  • Percent change vs. baseline
  • Inequalities (years above a threshold)
  • Trend graphing

Materials

  • Spreadsheet software (Excel, Google Sheets, Numbers)
  • NOAA Honolulu climate data 1995–present
  • Calculator (built-in)

Step-by-Step

  1. Download data. Pull NOAA monthly temperature and rainfall for Honolulu, 1995–2025.
  2. Build a clean table. One row per year, columns for annual mean temp, annual rainfall, hottest month, wettest month.
  3. Decadal statistics. Compute mean, median, range, and IQR for each decade (1995–2004, 2005–2014, 2015–2024).
  4. Percent change. Compute %-change in mean temp from 1995 to most recent year.
  5. Inequality analysis. Identify all years where mean temp > (1995 mean + 1 °C). What proportion of recent years exceeds this?
  6. Trend chart. Line chart of annual mean temperature with trend line.
  7. Dashboard. Combine on one sheet: stats table, trend chart, key callouts (e.g., "coolest year," "hottest year").
  8. Summary. 1-page write-up: is Honolulu warming? Cite three pieces of evidence from your dashboard.

Deliverable

Interactive spreadsheet with dashboard sheet + 1-page summary memo answering "is Honolulu warming?"

Sustainable Hale Design Project (Word-Problems Capstone)

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · B · C · D

Math Content

  • Area (rectangles, triangles)
  • Surface area (walls, roof)
  • Volume (room capacity for cooling)
  • Word problems integrating all topics

Materials

  • Graph paper
  • Ruler
  • Calculator
  • Cardboard or paper for 1:24 scale model
  • Reference photos of traditional Hawaiian hale

Step-by-Step

  1. Research the hale. Look up traditional Hawaiian hale construction: floor plan, pili-grass thatching, ohia post framing, roof pitch.
  2. Design a 1-room hale. Plan view, ~3 × 4 m floor. Draw to scale on graph paper.
  3. Calculate floor area. A = l × w.
  4. Calculate wall surface area. Four walls; subtract door opening.
  5. Roof pitch geometry. Roof is two triangles + two trapezoids. Compute roof surface area.
  6. Volume. Compute room volume for passive cooling calculations.
  7. Material list. Estimate pili grass needed (kg per m² of roof × roof area), ohia poles (count and total length).
  8. Build 1:24 scale model. Use cardboard. Scale every dimension: real m × (1/24) × 100 = model cm.
  9. Capstone word problem. One page: "If hot air rises and a hale holds 36 m³ of air at 30 °C, and the door area is 1.5 m², how long until air turnover at typical Hawaiian trade-wind speeds?" Solve.

Deliverable

Scaled blueprint + physical 1:24 model + material list + 1-page capstone word problem with full solution.
