COOKING CLASS · SYLLABUS

Plate Lunch Around the World

Life skills through world cuisines — with aloha at the center.

InstructorDr. Peter Park
Grades6 – 8
DurationTwo Quarters · 18 Sessions
Class Length45 – 55 minutes

Course Overview

This two-quarter elective builds practical life skills through global and local food culture. Quarter 2 takes students on a culinary journey across 9 countries — exploring recipes, traditions, and techniques from around the world. Quarter 4 dedicates a full 9 sessions to Hawaiian culture, tracing the islands’ food story from ancient Native Hawaiian traditions to today’s multicultural ‘local food’ identity. Every session connects cooking to real-world life skills students will carry well beyond the classroom.

Core Life Skills Woven Throughout

  • Kitchen & food safety — hygiene, knife safety, heat awareness
  • Measurement and math — fractions, conversions, doubling/halving recipes
  • Reading and following instructions — recipes as step-by-step procedures
  • Teamwork and communication — working in small cooking groups
  • Time management — preparing multiple components to finish together
  • Cultural literacy and respect — history, geography, and traditions behind each dish
  • Budgeting basics — cost-per-serving calculations in select lessons
  • Clean-up and responsibility — leaving the kitchen as you found it

Course Structure

The course runs over School Quarters 2 and 4, with 9 sessions each quarter (approximately 45–55 minutes per session). Each quarter concludes with a culminating showcase session where students present and share a dish of their choosing.

QUARTER 2 Foundations & Flavors From the Americas, Europe & Asia
1🇺🇸 Welcome to the Kitchen
RegionAmerican · Universal Life SkillKitchen safety, hygiene, and tool identification RecipeClassic Grilled Cheese Sandwich ConceptsKitchen safety rules, food handling basics, tool names
Learning Objectives
  • Identify and safely use basic kitchen tools (knives, cutting boards, pans)
  • Practice proper handwashing and food hygiene
  • Read a recipe from start to finish before beginning
  • Cook a simple stovetop recipe using butter and heat control

Instructor note: Establish group norms; assign kitchen roles (chef, sous chef, cleaner, presenter).

2🇲🇽 Flavors of Mexico
RegionMexico Life SkillKnife skills — chopping, dicing, mincing safely RecipeFresh Tomato Salsa & Guacamole ConceptsKnife safety, flavor balancing, cultural geography
Learning Objectives
  • Practice the ‘claw grip’ knife technique for safe chopping
  • Understand how acid (lime juice) affects flavor and texture
  • Learn about Mexican culinary history and the role of corn and chili
  • Taste and adjust seasoning — salt, acid, heat

Instructor note: No-cook recipe great for building confidence early in the course.

3🇮🇹 Pasta Night in Italy
RegionItaly Life SkillMeasuring accurately; boiling water safely RecipeCold Pasta Primavera Salad with Italian Dressing ConceptsMeasurement (fractions, ml/cups), stovetop safety, Mediterranean diet
Learning Objectives
  • Measure dry and liquid ingredients using standard and metric units
  • Safely boil pasta and test for doneness
  • Explore Italy’s regional food traditions and the Mediterranean diet
  • Practice draining hot liquids safely using a colander

Instructor note: Tie in math — doubling the recipe for a class of 30 vs. 15.

4🇯🇵 Rice Culture in Japan
RegionJapan Life SkillRice cooking ratios; precise portioning and shaping RecipeOnigiri (Japanese Rice Balls) with Furikake ConceptsRatios, portions, Japanese food culture
Learning Objectives
  • Cook rice using the absorption method (water-to-rice ratios)
  • Shape and portion rice balls by hand using wet and salted hands
  • Learn the cultural significance of onigiri in Japanese daily life
  • Discuss umami as the ‘fifth taste.’

Instructor note: Students can fill with tuna-mayo or pickled plum (umeboshi).

5🇮🇳 Spices & Bread from India
RegionIndia Life SkillKneading dough; understanding spice blends RecipeChapati Flatbread & Mango Lassi ConceptsDough chemistry basics, dry heat cooking, Indian geography
Learning Objectives
  • Mix and knead dough to the correct consistency
  • Cook flatbread on a dry skillet, recognizing visual doneness cues
  • Blend a yogurt-based drink (lassi) — explore dairy and probiotics
  • Learn how spices like turmeric and cumin have historical trade value

Instructor note: Great lesson on gluten development — connect to science.

6🇨🇳 Wok Cooking in China
RegionChina Life SkillHigh-heat cooking; mise en place RecipeVegetable Fried Rice ConceptsMise en place, high-heat safety, Chinese food culture
Learning Objectives
  • Practice mise en place — all ingredients prepped before heat goes on
  • Use high heat and quick tossing techniques safely
  • Learn the Chinese culinary philosophy of balance (yin and yang in cooking)
  • Understand why day-old rice works best for fried rice (moisture content)

Instructor note: Prepare rice the day before if possible. Great teamwork session.

7🇫🇷 The Art of French Crêpes
RegionFrance Life SkillBatter consistency; pan temperature control RecipeSweet Crêpes with Strawberry Jam & Powdered Sugar ConceptsBatter mixing, heat management, French culture
Learning Objectives
  • Whisk a smooth, lump-free batter and rest it before cooking
  • Control pan temperature to avoid burning thin batter
  • Learn about French culinary traditions (Mardi Gras crêpe traditions)
  • Practice flipping — patience and wrist technique

Instructor note: Allow students to experiment with fillings — a class favorite.

8🇪🇹 Ethiopian Stew & Flatbread
RegionEthiopia Life SkillSlow cooking and layering flavors over time RecipeRed Lentil Stew (Misir Wat) with Injera-Style Flatbread ConceptsSpice blooming, legume cooking, East African food culture
Learning Objectives
  • Build flavor by blooming spices in oil (berbere spice blend)
  • Cook lentils from dry to tender — observe texture changes
  • Learn about injera (teff flatbread) and communal eating in Ethiopian culture
  • Discuss protein sources in plant-based diets

Instructor note: Simulated injera can be made with buckwheat or whole wheat flour.

9🌍 Quarter 2 Food Festival
RegionStudent Choice Life SkillPlanning, presenting, and explaining a dish to an audience RecipeStudent-selected dish from any Q2 culture ConceptsCulminating performance task — all Q2 skills
Learning Objectives
  • Independently (or in pairs) prepare a dish from Quarter 2
  • Write a 3-sentence cultural summary to share with the class
  • Practice plating and presentation techniques
  • Reflect: What life skill did you grow the most this quarter?

Instructor note: Invite parents or other classes if space allows. Students vote on favorite dish.

QUARTER 4 Aloha Kitchen Hawaiian Culture & Cuisine — from traditional roots to modern local food
10🌺 Aloha & the Roots of Hawaiian Food
RegionTraditional Hawaii (Native Hawaiian) Life SkillKitchen safety review; food as culture and identity RecipePoi (Taro Paste) Tasting & Fresh Fruit Platter with Haupia Dipping Sauce ConceptsNative Hawaiian history, taro/poi cultural significance, fermentation basics
Learning Objectives
  • Learn about taro (kalo) as the sacred foundation of Native Hawaiian culture and diet
  • Understand ‘āina (land) and its connection to food sovereignty
  • Taste poi and discuss how fermentation changes flavor over time
  • Make a simple haupia (coconut pudding) sauce — practice stovetop stirring and heat control

Instructor note: Set a respectful, curious tone for the unit. Invite students to share any Hawaiian connections they have.

11🌺 The Imu & Kalua Cooking
RegionTraditional Hawaii Life SkillUnderstanding slow cooking and heat transfer RecipeSlow-Cooker Kalua Pork with Cabbage & Steamed White Rice ConceptsHeat transfer methods, slow cooking, Hawaiian luau traditions
Learning Objectives
  • Learn about the traditional imu (underground oven) and how it uses steam and heat
  • Use a slow cooker as a classroom analogy for imu cooking — low heat over long time
  • Understand how salt, liquid smoke, and time transform a tough cut of meat
  • Connect to the luau tradition — food in Hawaiian celebrations and community

Instructor note: Start slow cooker at the beginning of class so it’s ready to taste by end. Prepare rice separately.

12🌺 Poke — Hawaii’s Original Bowl
RegionTraditional & Modern Hawaii Life SkillKnife skills — precise dicing; food safety with fresh fish RecipeShoyu Ahi Poke Bowl with Sushi Rice & Toppings ConceptsKnife skills, raw fish safety, Hawaiian fishing culture, food globalization
Learning Objectives
  • Dice ahi tuna (or tofu) into uniform cubes — reinforce knife precision and safety
  • Make a shoyu-sesame marinade and understand how it flavors the fish
  • Learn how poke evolved from a traditional Hawaiian fishermen’s snack to a global food trend
  • Discuss food safety: temperature, freshness, and safe handling of raw fish

Instructor note: Use sushi-grade ahi if budget allows; firm tofu works as a safe, delicious alternative.

13🏝️ Spam Musubi & the Local Food Story
RegionLocal Hawaii (WWII Era to Present) Life SkillShaping and forming food; food history and cultural adaptation RecipeSpam Musubi with Furikake & Soy Glaze ConceptsFood history (WWII), cultural adaptation, Hawaii’s multicultural identity
Learning Objectives
  • Cook and glaze Spam slices — manage browning with a sweet soy sauce
  • Mold musubi using a musubi press or DIY cardboard mold
  • Learn the history of Spam in Hawaii — WWII military rations and local adoption
  • Discuss how immigrant and military cultures shaped Hawaii’s unique ‘local food’ identity

Instructor note: One of the most beloved local snacks — fun, easy, and universally enjoyed.

14🏝️ The Plate Lunch — Hawaii’s Comfort Food
RegionLocal Hawaii (Japanese, Korean, Chinese, Portuguese influences) Life SkillBalanced meal planning; understanding portion sizes RecipeLoco Moco (Rice, Hamburger Patty, Egg & Brown Gravy) ConceptsSafe meat cooking temperatures, roux-based sauces, Hawaii food history
Learning Objectives
  • Cook a hamburger patty to safe internal temperature — use a meat thermometer
  • Make a simple brown gravy from scratch (roux method)
  • Fry an egg to over-easy — practice gentle heat and timing
  • Learn about the loco moco’s origin in Hilo, Hawaii and the plate lunch tradition

Instructor note: Great session for discussing balanced nutrition vs. comfort food — both have a place.

15🏝️ Saimin — Hawaii’s Soul Noodle Soup
RegionLocal Hawaii (Japanese, Chinese, Filipino origins) Life SkillBroth-making and layering umami; managing multiple components RecipeSaimin Noodle Soup with Char Siu, Fishcake & Green Onion ConceptsBroth-making, noodle cooking, plantation era history, cultural fusion
Learning Objectives
  • Build a dashi-style broth with dried ingredients — understand umami depth
  • Cook fresh saimin noodles and time them with the broth
  • Learn how saimin was born from the blending of Japanese, Chinese, and Filipino noodle traditions on sugar plantations
  • Discuss Hawaii’s plantation era and how it shaped the islands’ multicultural food scene

Instructor note: Saimin is uniquely Hawaiian — not found anywhere else. A great pride moment for the unit.

16🏝️ Shave Ice, Malasadas & Hawaiian Sweets
RegionLocal Hawaii (Portuguese & Japanese influences) Life SkillSimple syrup making; frying dough safely; flavor combinations RecipeMalasadas (Portuguese Fried Doughnuts) with Homemade Flavored Simple Syrups ConceptsYeast dough, deep frying safety, Portuguese immigration history, Japanese influence
Learning Objectives
  • Make a yeast dough, let it rise, and fry malasadas — observe gluten and yeast activity
  • Cook simple syrups in different flavors (passion fruit, strawberry, coconut)
  • Learn about the Portuguese contribution to Hawaii’s food culture (plantation worker immigration)
  • Discuss shave ice as a Japanese-inspired treat transformed into a Hawaiian icon

Instructor note: If frying is not available, malasadas can be baked. Simple syrups can be used on ice as a shave-ice substitute.

17🌺 Hawaiian Gardens — Taro, Sweet Potato & More
RegionTraditional & Modern Hawaii Life SkillRoasting and steaming root vegetables; farm-to-table RecipeRoasted Hawaiian Sweet Potato (Okinawan Purple) & Taro Chips with Hawaiian Sea Salt ConceptsRoot vegetable cooking, Hawaiian agriculture, food sovereignty, farm-to-table
Learning Objectives
  • Roast and steam Hawaiian root vegetables — observe texture and color changes
  • Make taro chips: slice thin, season, and bake or fry to crispy
  • Learn about the diversity of Hawaiian agriculture — Okinawan sweet potato, breadfruit, banana
  • Discuss the importance of local farming and food sovereignty in Hawaii today

Instructor note: Okinawan purple sweet potato is a stunning visual — students love the color reveal when sliced.

18🌺 Aloha Feast — Q4 Hawaiian Showcase
RegionHawaii — All Traditions Life SkillMeal planning, presentation, cultural storytelling, aloha spirit RecipeStudent-selected Hawaiian dish from any Q4 session ConceptsCulminating performance task — all Q4 Hawaiian culture and cooking skills
Learning Objectives
  • Independently or in pairs, prepare a dish from Quarter 4
  • Write a recipe card and a cultural reflection: what does this dish mean to Hawaiian culture?
  • Present dish to class in the spirit of a luau — share, celebrate, and give thanks
  • Reflect: What did you learn about Hawaii that surprised you most?

Instructor note: Decorate the space, play Hawaiian music, and end the year with aloha. Award course completion certificates.

Assessment & Grading

Because this is a life skills elective, assessment focuses on growth, participation, and real-world skill demonstration — not traditional tests.

CategoryWeightHow It’s Measured
Participation & Teamwork 30% Engages actively in cooking group; supports teammates; cleans up
Skills Demonstrations 30% Correctly applies each session’s target skill (knife grip, measuring, heat control, etc.)
Recipe Reflection 20% Brief written or verbal reflection after each session — what did I learn? what would I do differently?
Food Festival / Showcase 20% Quarterly culminating task — dish quality, cultural summary, and presentation

Classroom Kitchen Rules & Safety

  • Always wash hands with soap for 20 seconds before handling any food.
  • Long hair must be tied back; loose clothing should be secured before approaching heat.
  • Never leave heat unattended — assign a dedicated ‘heat monitor’ in each group.
  • Knives go on the cutting board, never the edge of the counter or in a sink.
  • Report all cuts, burns, or spills to the instructor immediately.
  • Taste food with a clean spoon — never double-dip.
  • Clean as you go — wipe counters, wash tools, and sweep before the session ends.
  • Respect dietary restrictions and allergies of every classmate — always ask before sharing.
  • No running, horseplay, or distractions near hot surfaces or sharp tools.
  • Food made in class is for everyone — be generous and inclusive.

Materials & Supply Notes

Each session requires approximately $20–$40 in ingredients for a class of 20–25 students. Below are essential equipment items needed for the course.

  • Chef’s knives (1 per 2 students) and cutting boards (color-coded by use)
  • Medium and large mixing bowls
  • Measuring cups and measuring spoons (dry and liquid sets)
  • 12″ non-stick skillets / frying pans (at least 4–6 for class)
  • Medium saucepans with lids
  • Colanders, graters, and whisks
  • Baking sheets and oven mitts
  • Rice cooker or stovetop pot for rice sessions
  • Blender or immersion blender (for smoothies and sauces)
  • Aprons and disposable gloves
  • Basic pantry: olive oil, salt, pepper, soy sauce, rice, pasta, canned tomatoes

Ingredient lists for each individual session are available as separate handouts.
