🌍 Why the Metric System?

Only three countries in the world — the United States, Liberia, and Myanmar — don't use the metric system every day. But scientists everywhere use it. Scientists in Tokyo, Berlin, and Honolulu need to compare data using the same set of units. Those units are called SI units, and they are metric.

Big Idea: Numbers are the language of science. To compare data around the world, we all need to speak the same language.

➡️ Simple Conversions

To convert one metric unit to another, just count how many steps you move on the KHDUDCM staircase — that's how many places the decimal point slides.

Example 1: 500 centimeters → meters

  • Start at c, move to U = 2 steps left
  • Slide the decimal 2 places left: 500. → 5.00
  • Answer: 5 m

Example 2: 4,000 m → km

  • m → k = 3 steps left → 4.000 → 4 km

Example 3: 45.5 L → mL

  • L → mL = 3 steps right → 45,500 → 45,500 mL
Warning: This decimal-slide trick only works for regular units. For units that are squared (cm²) or cubed (cm³), use unit analysis instead.

🔢 Precision & Significant Digits

Which number is most precise?

  • 0.2
  • 0.21
  • 0.20788
  • 0.20787958

The bottom one looks the most precise — but we can only accept that precision if the tool we used was actually that precise. If you measured with a ruler that only shows tenths, then 0.2 is your real answer. Don't just copy every digit your calculator gives you.

Rule: Round your final answer to the precision of your least precise measurement (usually the thousandths place is plenty for a middle school science lab).

🔬 Scientific Notation

Scientists sometimes deal with very large or very small numbers — the distance from Oahu to the Big Island (~300,000 m) or the mass of a mosquito (~0.00000003 g). Scientific notation lets us write them cleanly.

3.0 × 10⁵ m = 300,000 m

Positive exponent → move the decimal right (big number). Negative exponent → move the decimal left (small number).

3.0 × 10⁻⁸ g = 0.00000003 g

If your calculator shows 4.587E4, that means 4.587 × 10⁴ = 45,870. Never write "E" on your paper — convert it first.

🧮 Unit Analysis (Factor-Label)

Unit analysis is the universal way to convert between units. It works for any units — metric or not, squared or cubed, English to metric, whatever — because it uses fractions equal to 1.

Any equality (like 60 minutes = 1 hour) can be written as a fraction that equals 1:

60 min / 1 hr = 1   OR   1 hr / 60 min = 1

The Recipe

Start with what you have, including its units.
Multiply by one or more "unit factors" (fractions equal to 1).
Flip each fraction so the unit you don't want cancels.
The unit you keep is the one you want.

Example: How many minutes in 2 hours?

2 hr × (60 min / 1 hr) = 120 min

The "hr" on top cancels the "hr" on the bottom, leaving minutes. ✓

Example: How many cm in a mile?

Given: 1 inch = 2.54 cm; 1609 m = 1 mile

1 mile × (1609 m / 1 mi) × (100 cm / 1 m) = 160,900 cm
