PARENT & ADMIN

Frequently Asked Questions

What grade levels is this for?

Rising 4th and 5th graders. Mixed-level crews work well because older students mentor younger.

How many students per session?

12–20 explorers, in crews of 3–4. One head teacher plus one assistant works best.

How much math vs. play?

Roughly 60% direct math practice, 40% story/game framing. The framing is the carrot — the math is the meal.

What if a student misses a day?

Each day's mission stands alone. They can complete the week's boss challenge with crew support.

Is there homework?

It's summer — minimal. One short adventure journal entry per night.

What does my child take home?

Journal, explorer pack, five map pieces, five mission badges, plus the Captain badge.

STORY TIME · 10 MINUTES

📖 25 Story Chapters — One Per Day

Each chapter sets up the day's math problem inside the adventure. Read aloud at the start of class to launch the kids' imagination — and the math follows naturally.

🕙 How to Run a 10-Minute Story Time

Each chapter is short (~280 words, ~3 min to read aloud). The remaining minutes are for discussion that primes the day's mini-lesson. You're not testing comprehension — you're setting the table.

🎤 0–3 min · ReadRead the chapter aloud with expression. Pause on character names. Let the crew picture it.
💭 3–6 min · ReactUse the discussion prompt at the end. "Has this ever happened to you?" "What would you do?" Keep it open.
🔗 6–8 min · ConnectSurface the day's math: "What math do the crew need to solve this?" Let kids name it before you do.
🚀 8–10 min · LaunchTransition: "Today, YOU become the crew. Same problem. Same skill." Roll into the mini-lesson.

🏝️ Week 1 · Lost Island of Numbers

5 chapters — one for each day's adventure
🏝️
W1·D1 Counting the Shipwreck Supplies Place value
The storm hit at midnight. By morning, Captain Parker and the young crew — Timo, Mackenzie, and Nelly — wash up on a beach they've never seen. Around them lie the broken pieces of their ship, plus dozens of barrels, sacks, and crates that survived the wreck.

"First job: take stock," says Captain Parker. "We can't ration what we can't count."

Timo finds a pile of coconuts so tall it reaches his knees. Mackenzie pours out a sack of beans — they cover a square meter of sand. Nelly opens a barrel and finds it filled to the brim with fishing hooks. Every supply is in numbers too big to count one at a time.

"What's the biggest number you've written, Nelly?" Captain Parker asks.

Nelly thinks. "Like... two hundred?"

"Today you'll write six-digit numbers. Hundred-thousands. Maybe more." She kneels and draws place columns in the sand: ___ ___ ___ , ___ ___ ___ . "Each spot means something different. The 4 here means four. But the 4 over there means four hundred thousand. Same digit, very different value."

Mackenzie stares at the coconuts. "If I miscount by one in the wrong place, that's a hundred coconuts missing."

"Exactly. On this island, place value is survival."

💬 Discuss: What numbers do we write in real life? Phone numbers? Prices? Years? How many digits is the biggest you've ever seen?
W1·D2 Reading the Tide Rounding
Day 2: the crew needs fresh water. The stream is on the other side of jagged tide pools — only safe to cross when the tide is below 3,000 millimeters.

Captain Parker checks the tide gauge: 3,672 mm.

"Round it to the nearest thousand," she says.

Timo squints. "3,672 is closer to 4,000 than 3,000. So it rounds up. Too high to cross."

They wait. An hour later: 2,847 mm. Mackenzie thinks. "Closest thousand is 3,000. Still risky."

Another half hour. 2,498. Nelly checks. "Nearest thousand is 2,000. Way below 3,000. Safe!"

Captain Parker nods. "When you have to decide fast, the exact number doesn't matter. The rounded number does. Is the tide HIGH (around 4,000)? Or LOW (around 2,000)? That decision saves time — and lives."

Mackenzie leads them across. The rocks are slippery but stable. They reach the stream and fill the canteens. As they walk back, Timo asks, "What if I rounded 3,672 the wrong way?"

Captain Parker shrugs. "Then maybe we'd cross when we shouldn't. The sea wouldn't forgive that."

Mackenzie looks at the rocks one last time. "Rounding isn't just a math trick. It's how we move through the world."

💬 Discuss: When do adults round in real life? Money? Time? Driving distances? Why not always use the exact number?
W1·D3 Building the Shelter Addition & subtraction
Night is coming on Day 3. The crew needs shelter — and that means palm logs.

Captain Parker counts what they have: 142 logs. She estimates they need 348 to build a safe wall against the wind.

"How many more do we cut?" Nelly asks.

Timo writes it out: 348 − 142 = ?

He works through it carefully: borrowing where he needs to, lining up the columns. 348 − 142 = 206.

"206 more logs," Mackenzie says. "We've got two hours of daylight. Let's split up."

They chop until the sun drops. By dusk, the count is 348 logs. They start building.

As they stack, Mackenzie frowns. "What if the wind tonight is stronger than we thought? We might lose some before morning."

"How many can we afford to lose?" Captain Parker asks.

Timo thinks. "We need 348 minimum. We have 348. So we can't afford to lose ANY."

"Then let's cut 30 more, just in case."

They work past sunset. By the time stars appear, they have 378 logs. That night, the wind howls. By morning, six logs have rolled off the beach. 378 − 6 = 372. Still enough.

"Subtraction tells us the gap," Captain Parker says. "Addition tells us how things grow. Together, they're how we PLAN."

💬 Discuss: When do you add or subtract in real life? Allowance? Lunch money? Time until vacation?
W1·D4 Crab Catch Multiplication Multiplication
Day 4: the food problem. The crew has been eating coconuts for three days. They need protein.

Captain Parker leads them to a tidal reef teeming with crabs.

"We can lay 6 traps. Each trap should catch about 24 crabs per night."

Timo pulls out his pencil. "6 traps times 24 crabs each. That's 6 × 24."

He tries counting: 24, 48, 72, 96, 120, 144. "144 crabs?"

Captain Parker nods. "Multiplication is faster than counting. The bigger the numbers, the more time it saves."

Mackenzie does it differently. "I can split 24 into 20 + 4. So 6 × 24 = 6 × 20 + 6 × 4 = 120 + 24 = 144."

"Same answer, different path," Captain Parker says. "Both work."

They set the traps. The next morning: 137 crabs. Close to the estimate, but not exact.

"Multiplication helps us PREDICT," Mackenzie says. "We thought 144. We got 137. Off by 7. That's planning — estimates that are close enough to act on."

That night they eat real food for the first time since the shipwreck. Nelly gives a satisfied burp.

Captain Parker laughs. "Multiplication just fed us. Remember that. Math isn't decoration. Math is dinner."

💬 Discuss: When do adults need to predict using multiplication? At a restaurant? On a road trip? When packing for a class trip?
W1·D5 The Compass Quest BOSS · All Week 1
The crew has supplies. Shelter. Food. But they're still stranded.

On Day 5, Captain Parker pulls out the only thing left from the captain's quarters: a torn map. It shows a legendary compass hidden somewhere on the island, broken into 5 pieces. Each piece is locked behind a math riddle from this week's lessons.

"The compass is our way home," Captain Parker says. "But you have to earn it. Each piece is a different skill. Place value. Rounding. Adding and subtracting. Multiplying. You'll need them all."

The crew looks at each other. Timo cracks his knuckles. Mackenzie rolls up her sleeves. Nelly grins.

"We've been training for this all week."

Captain Parker holds up the map. "When you face a problem, don't panic at the size. Ask: WHAT KIND OF PROBLEM IS THIS? Then pick the skill. Then solve."

They follow the map into the jungle. The first riddle is carved into a stone tree: a place value puzzle. Timo solves it — the tree splits open, revealing the first compass piece.

The second piece is hidden in a cliff — a rounding puzzle. Mackenzie cracks it.

The third lies under a rock — an addition story. Nelly gets it.

The fourth needs multiplication of two-digit numbers. The crew works together.

The fifth is the hardest: a multi-step problem combining EVERYTHING.

They take a deep breath. Then they solve.

The compass assembles itself in Captain Parker's hand. It glows.

"To the temple," she says. "Our journey just started."

💬 Discuss: What's a skill you've been practicing all week that you feel ready to test? Why does combining skills feel harder than learning them separately?

🏛️ Week 2 · Temple of Fractions

5 chapters — one for each day's adventure
🏛️
W2·D1 Stone of Equivalence Equivalent fractions
The compass leads off the island. The crew sails for two days and lands at a green coast covered in vines. Through the jungle they find an enormous stone temple half-buried in moss.

On the door: 12 carved stones, each with a fraction. 1/2. 2/4. 4/8. 3/6. 1/3. 2/6. 3/4. 6/8. 1/4. 2/8. 5/10. 4/12.

The riddle below reads: "Match the stones that mean the same. Open the door."

Mackenzie stares. "1/2 and 2/4. Those LOOK different, but they're the same amount."

Captain Parker nods. "Half a pizza. Two slices out of four. Same pizza."

They start matching.

1/2 = 2/4 = 4/8 — the stones glow.
1/3 = 2/6 — they glow brighter.
3/4 = 6/8 — another match.
1/4 = 2/8 — the last pair.

The door rumbles open. Cool air rushes out.

"Equivalent fractions look different but ARE the same," Nelly says. "Like wearing different clothes but still being you."

Mackenzie laughs. "Two-fourths and one-half are wearing different outfits to the same party."

Captain Parker smiles. "There's a rule. Multiply (or divide) the top AND bottom by the same number. The value stays the same. You're just renaming it."

She picks up 1/2 and walks through the door. Nelly notes: "2 × 4 = 8. So 1 × 4 = 4. That's how 1/2 becomes 4/8."

"Yes. Memorize that move. The Temple loves it."

💬 Discuss: Can you think of two different ways to write the same fraction? What about money — is 50 cents the same as 2 quarters? Why?
W2·D2 Hall of Mirrors Comparing fractions
Inside the temple is a long hallway lined with mirrors. As the crew walks in, the mirrors twist their reflections — tall, short, wide, thin. To exit, they must order four fractions from smallest to largest.

The fractions written on the mirrors: 2/3, 1/2, 5/8, 7/12.

Timo studies them. "1/2 is the smallest, right?"

"Let's check," Mackenzie says. "1/2 = 6/12. And 7/12 is just one twelfth bigger. So 7/12 is right after 1/2."

They make a common denominator: 24.
1/2 = 12/24.
2/3 = 16/24.
5/8 = 15/24.
7/12 = 14/24.

Mackenzie writes: 12 < 14 < 15 < 16.

So: 1/2 < 7/12 < 5/8 < 2/3.

They speak the order aloud. The mirrors stop twisting. The next door slides open.

"Fractions can be tricky to compare," Captain Parker says. "The numbers on top don't tell the whole story. Neither do the numbers on bottom. You have to TRANSLATE them all into the same language — a common denominator. Then they line up."

"Or," Mackenzie adds, "you can use a benchmark. 1/2 is the easiest one. Bigger than 1/2 or smaller? That tells you a lot."

Nelly grins. "I'm going to use the 1/2 trick from now on."

💬 Discuss: Which would you rather have — 2/3 of a pizza or 3/4 of a pizza? How do you decide?
W2·D3 Adding Pool Adding fractions
Past the mirrors is a sacred pool, dry and empty. To fill it, the crew must add the right fractions of water from special barrels.

The temple's instructions, carved in stone: "Fill the pool to exactly 1 whole. Use the barrels."

Three barrels stand by the pool, labeled 1/2 full, 1/3 full, and 1/6 full.

Nelly studies them. "Can we just dump them in?"

Mackenzie works it out on a sheet of papyrus. "To add 1/2 + 1/3, we need a common denominator. The smallest is 6. So 1/2 becomes 3/6. And 1/3 becomes 2/6."

She writes:
3/6 + 2/6 + 1/6 = 6/6 = 1 whole.

"Exactly enough! Pour them all in!"

Timo tips the first barrel. The pool starts to fill. Then the second. Then the third. The water rises perfectly to the brim and stops.

A bell rings. A door behind the pool opens.

"Different denominators are like different languages," Captain Parker says. "You can't add words in different languages until you translate them all into one. Same with fractions. Same denominator first, THEN add the tops."

Mackenzie nods. "And the bottoms stay the same. We're just counting how many of those pieces we have."

"That's the part kids forget," Nelly says. "We don't add 6 + 6 = 12 on the bottom. 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6, not 5/12."

Captain Parker grins. "You won't forget. Not after the Pool."

💬 Discuss: If I ate 1/2 of one pizza and 1/4 of another, how many pizzas did I eat total?
W2·D4 Mixing Chamber Subtracting fractions
Beyond the pool is the alchemist's chamber. Bottles, beakers, glowing liquids. The walls hum with energy.

On a stone tablet, the puzzle: "From a 2 1/4 cup mixture, remove exactly 5/6 cup. What is left? Pour the answer into the empty flask. Be precise."

Timo frowns. "Subtracting from a mixed number is tricky."

Mackenzie thinks. "Convert 2 1/4 to a fraction. 2 1/4 = 9/4."

She writes: 9/4 − 5/6 = ?

"Common denominator. 4 and 6 both go into 12. So 9/4 becomes 27/12. And 5/6 becomes 10/12."

27/12 − 10/12 = 17/12.

"That's an improper fraction. Convert back to mixed: 17/12 = 1 5/12."

She pours out exactly 5/6 cup from the big beaker into a measuring cup. What's left in the big beaker is 1 5/12 cups. She pours that into the empty flask.

A bell rings. The chamber's secret compartment slides open. Inside is a glowing crystal — a clue for the next room.

"Subtracting fractions is the same as adding," Captain Parker says. "Match denominators. Then subtract the tops. Bottoms stay the same."

"And mixed numbers can be scary," Mackenzie adds, "but converting to an improper fraction first usually makes it easier."

Nelly nods. "9/4 looks weirder than 2 1/4, but it's easier to subtract from."

💬 Discuss: If you had 1 1/2 hours of homework and you finished 3/4 of an hour, how much is left?
W2·D5 Sacred Relic BOSS · All Week 2
Deep in the temple, behind every chamber, the crew finds the Sacred Relic — a glowing jewel resting on an altar. But to claim it, they must solve a final fraction puzzle that combines every skill from this week.

The altar's stone tablet lists the challenge:
· Find an equivalent fraction.
· Compare two fractions.
· Add fractions with different denominators.
· Subtract a mixed number.
· And one bonus: figure out how much of the relic each crew member earned.

Captain Parker looks at her crew.

"You've learned a LOT this week. Equivalence. Comparing. Adding. Subtracting. Mixed numbers. Today, you face all of them at once."

Nelly gulps. "What if I forget which is which?"

"Read each problem twice. Ask: WHAT KIND OF FRACTION QUESTION IS THIS? Name the skill before you compute. Once you know the kind, you know the move."

She points at the first riddle. "Equivalent? Multiply or divide top and bottom by the same number."

"Comparing? Common denominator."

"Adding or subtracting? Common denominator. Then operate on the tops."

"Mixed number? Convert to improper first."

The crew nods. They each take a problem. They work in parallel.

Within minutes, all four problems are solved. The Sacred Relic lifts off the altar and hovers above the crew, glowing brighter.

"You've earned this," Captain Parker says. "Take it. We'll need it where we're going next — the Cave of Decimals."

💬 Discuss: What's the trickiest fraction question you've seen this week? Which kind of problem do you feel most ready for today?

💎 Week 3 · Cave of Decimals

5 chapters — one for each day's adventure
💎
W3·D1 Glowing Crystals Decimal place value
The Sacred Relic from the temple is actually a map — a crystal map. It glows in the dark, pointing the way to a mountain.

After three days of travel, the crew finds the entrance to a vast cave. They light torches and step inside.

The walls glow softly. Stalactites drip overhead. As they walk deeper, the floor becomes covered in tiny glowing crystals.

Nelly picks one up. It's smooth, warm, and labeled with a number: 4.305.

"What does that mean?" he asks.

"That's its glow value," Captain Parker says. "Each digit means something. Let's read it."

Timo studies it carefully. "The 4 is in the ones place. The decimal point separates whole from parts. The 3 is in the tenths place — three-tenths of one. The 0 is in the hundredths — zero hundredths. The 5 is in the thousandths."

"Right," Captain Parker says. "After the decimal point, every place is a fraction of one. Tenths means one-tenth. Hundredths means one-hundredth. Thousandths means one-thousandth."

Mackenzie picks up a crystal labeled 0.07. "This one has nothing in the tenths place, but a 7 in hundredths. So it's seven-hundredths. Pretty dim."

Nelly holds up another: 7.0. "This is just seven. The zero in tenths doesn't add anything."

"Now you're getting it," Captain Parker says. "Decimals are tricky because the placeholders MATTER even when they're zeros."

💬 Discuss: Where do you see decimals in real life? Prices? Sports stats? Body temperatures? Why do we use decimals instead of fractions in those places?
W3·D2 Crystal Comparison Comparing decimals
Deeper in the cave, the crew finds two crystals side by side on a ledge. One is labeled 0.27, the other 0.3.

"Which is brighter?" Timo asks.

Nelly immediately says: "0.27! Because 27 is bigger than 3!"

Mackenzie raises an eyebrow. "Watch this." She pulls out a hundredths grid — a 10x10 square divided into 100 little squares. She colors 27 of them in red and labels the grid 0.27. Then she colors another grid with 30 squares (since 0.3 = 30 hundredths) and labels it 0.3.

Nelly's eyes widen. "0.3 takes up MORE of the grid! It's brighter!"

"0.3 = 0.30 = 30 hundredths," Mackenzie says. "27 hundredths is LESS than 30 hundredths. Decimals are SNEAKY."

Captain Parker nods. "When comparing decimals, you can't just look at the number of digits. You have to line up the decimal point and add zeros to make the lengths equal. THEN compare like regular numbers."

She holds up the two crystals. "Same trick: 0.27 and 0.30. Tenths: 2 vs 3. Three is bigger. 0.3 wins."

They place the brighter crystal (0.3) into a slot on the wall. The wall hums. A passage opens to the next chamber.

Nelly shakes his head. "I'll never trust a decimal at face value again."

"Smart kid," Captain Parker says. "Now you understand them."

💬 Discuss: Which is more — $0.5 or $0.45? Why is this tricky? Why do prices add a trailing zero like $4.50 instead of $4.5?
W3·D3 Combining Crystals Adding decimals
The crew finds three crystals at a junction. A glowing slot on the wall reads: "Combine the crystals' values to exactly 1.00 to open the next passage."

The crystals: 0.34, 0.21, and 0.45.

Mackenzie stacks them mentally. "Adding decimals is just like adding regular numbers — but you HAVE to line up the decimal point. Otherwise everything's off."

She writes vertically:
  0.34
  0.21
+0.45
−−−−
  1.00

"Exactly one!" she says.

She places the crystals in the slot. The wall hums. The passage opens.

"Why was the decimal point so important?" Nelly asks.

Captain Parker picks up Mackenzie's scratch paper. "Suppose you forgot to line them up. You might write 0.34 + 0.21 + 0.45 and end up with 100, or 10, or some random number. Lining up tells you exactly which place each digit lives in."

Mackenzie nods. "Hundredths under hundredths. Tenths under tenths. Ones under ones."

"And if some numbers have fewer decimal places than others?" Nelly asks.

"Add zeros! 0.5 + 0.34 = 0.50 + 0.34 = 0.84. The zero doesn't change the value, but it lines everything up."

Timo writes that down. "0.5 = 0.50 = 0.500. Same number. Different look."

"Right. Decimals are flexible. You can DRESS them in trailing zeros without changing their value."

💬 Discuss: When do adults add decimals? At the grocery store? When measuring? When making a recipe? Why does the decimal point matter so much for money?
W3·D4 Splitting Crystals Subtracting & powers of 10
In the next chamber sleeps a giant crystal beast. A wizard's note on a stone tablet says: "To pass, weaken the crystals by exactly 0.3 from each. Then use the multiplier stone to make tiny adjustments."

Mackenzie takes a crystal that reads 5.00. She subtracts 0.3.

She lines up the decimal:
  5.00
− 0.30

W4·D1 Cloud Architects Perimeter
The Crystal Heart unlocks a staircase made of clouds. The crew climbs and climbs, breaking through layers of mist until they reach the Sky Kingdom: a vast realm of floating islands, with herds of cloud-sheep roaming the meadows.

Mackenzie draws. "Perimeter is the distance ALL the way around. 8 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 26 meters. Or shortcut: 2 × (8 + 5) = 26."

W4·D2 Sky Pastures Area
Day 2 in the Sky Kingdom. The sheep are fenced in — but they need grass.

"How much grass to plant?" Mackenzie asks.

The Sky Lord says, "Cover the whole pasture floor."

The pasture is 8 m by 5 m.

"Perimeter tells you the BORDER," Mackenzie says. "Area tells you the INSIDE."

Timo does the math: 8 × 5 = 40 square meters of grass.

"Length times width gives you area for a rectangle," Captain Parker says. "Imagine the rectangle is made of 1-meter squares. 8 squares across, 5 rows. That's 40 squares total."

"Perimeter is about going around. Area is about filling in," Nelly says.

"Right. The units even tell you. Perimeter is just meters. Area is SQUARE meters. Because it's measured in little squares."

The Sky Lord shows them an L-shaped pasture. "What about this one?"

Mackenzie splits the L into two rectangles. The first is 6 × 4 = 24. The second is 3 × 2 = 6. Total = 30.

"Decomposing shapes is the trick," Captain Parker says. "Split into rectangles. Find each area. Add them up. Works for ANY shape made of rectangles."

Nelly doodles. "So if I want a fence, that's perimeter. If I want a rug, that's area."

"Exactly. Perimeter = how long the edge is. Area = how much space is inside."

Another to 135°. "OBTUSE. More than 90°, less than 180°. Wider."

And a flat line, 180°. "STRAIGHT. A flat line."

Mackenzie practices with the protractor. She measures the corner of a stone tablet: 90°. A chair leg's tilt: 105°. The branch of a cloud-tree: 40°.

"Angles tell direction," Captain Parker says. "A small angle change means a different destination entirely. Sailors live by them. Pilots live by them. Sky messengers live by them."

The Sky Lord nods. "Two more important things. Angles on a STRAIGHT line add to 180°. So if one angle is 50°, its partner is 130°. Angles in a TRIANGLE add to 180°. So if two are 60° and 70°, the third is 50°."

Nelly writes it down. "50° + 130° = 180°. 60° + 70° + 50° = 180°. Cool."

"With those rules and a protractor, you can solve almost any angle puzzle," Captain Parker says.

Nelly says, "Or in Battleship! 'D-7!'"

The Sky Lord nods. "All true. Coordinates are how we describe POSITION. Once you have them, you can find anywhere, measure distance, build shapes."

Mackenzie plots three more stars and connects them. A second triangle. Then a square. Then a star shape.

"The Sky Kingdom is a coordinate plane," Captain Parker says. "Every floating island has a position. That's why the kingdom holds together."

W5·D1 Stellar Data Bar graphs & mean

Day 2 at the space station. Mission Control shows the crew constellations on a huge curved screen.

"Stars in constellations have patterns. In this one, the Hunter, for every 3 yellow stars, there are 2 blue."

That's a ratio: 3:2.

Timo thinks. "9 yellow is 3 × 3. So blue should be 2 × 3 = 6 stars."

The screen shows the second constellation. They count: 6 blue stars. Match!

"Ratios let us predict," Captain Parker says. "Without them, we'd have to count every single star one at a time."

🏝️
1
10 min Game

Place Value War 🃏

Materials

  • 1 deck of cards (Ace-9 only, remove 10s and face cards = 36 cards)
  • 1 place-value mat per explorer

How to Run

  1. Shuffle the deck. Deal 6 cards to each of the 3 explorers.
  2. Each explorer picks 5 of their 6 cards and arranges them to make the BIGGEST 5-digit number on their mat.
  3. Going around the crew, each explorer reads their number aloud ("Forty-seven thousand, two hundred eighty-six").
  4. Whoever has the biggest number wins the round — earns 1 doubloon (tally).
  5. Reshuffle. Play best of 7 rounds.
Variation: Switch to making the SMALLEST number partway through — flips the strategy and forces them to rethink place value.

15 min Hands-On Build

Treasure Pile Estimator 💰

Materials

  • 4 tubs labeled "Pile A" through "Pile D"
  • Each tub: beans / beads / coins / pebbles (50–300 per tub)
  • 1 estimate-sheet per kid

How to Run

  1. Each explorer starts at a different pile.
  2. Without touching: WRITE your estimate on the sheet.
  3. Now count by grouping into 10s, then 100s. Write the actual count.
  4. Subtract: "off by ___."
  5. Rotate piles every ~3 min so each kid touches all 4 piles by the end.
What to watch for: Kids who never group — they're counting one at a time and losing track. Pause them with: "What if you made small groups of 10?"
