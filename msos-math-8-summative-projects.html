Two project-based summative assessments per quarter — each designed as a 1.5- to 2.5-week capstone tied to the math standards of the quarter and (where possible) directly aligned with a STEM science assessment. Each project lists the math content, materials, step-by-step instructions, and final deliverable. Q1

Lab Math & Number Foundation

Properties of Matter Forensics Lab

⏱ 1.5 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Density (D = m/V)
  • Significant figures
  • Percent error
  • Scientific notation

Materials

  • 5 numbered unknown samples (metals, plastics)
  • Digital scale (0.01 g)
  • Graduated cylinder (50 mL or 100 mL)
  • Reference table of densities
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Receive 5 unknown numbered samples. Do not be told what they are.
  2. Measure mass. 0.01 g precision.
  3. Measure volume. Water-displacement method for irregular shapes.
  4. Compute density. D = m / V with appropriate sig figs (limited by least-precise measurement).
  5. Match to reference table. Compare density to: aluminum 2.70, iron 7.87, copper 8.96, lead 11.34, brass ~8.4, PVC ~1.4, polyethylene ~0.95.
  6. Identify each sample. If two candidates are within 5% of each other, list both and propose a follow-up test (magnetic? color?).
  7. Compute percent error. For each sample, compare measured density to the accepted value of your identified material.
  8. Forensic report. 5-page lab report with photos, methods, calculations with sig figs, identification rationale, percent error analysis.

Deliverable

5-page forensic-style lab report.

Tsunami Travel-Time Calculator

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · D

Math Content

  • Square roots (v = √(g × h))
  • Scientific notation (Pacific distances)
  • Unit conversion (m/s ↔ km/h ↔ knots)

Materials

  • Spreadsheet software
  • Pacific bathymetry data (NOAA)
  • Distance reference (great-circle calculator)

Step-by-Step

  1. Learn the formula. Tsunami speed v = √(g × h) where g = 9.81 m/s² and h = ocean depth in meters.
  2. Build calculator skeleton. Spreadsheet with cells for source (lat/long), target (Hilo, Honolulu), and average depth along path.
  3. Plug in real depths. Average Pacific depth is 4,000 m. Speed ≈ √(9.81 × 4,000) ≈ 198 m/s. Verify your formula.
  4. Convert. m/s to km/h and to knots. Express speeds in scientific notation where helpful.
  5. Distance lookup. Use a great-circle calculator for source-to-Hilo distance.
  6. Travel time. Time = distance ÷ speed.
  7. Validate against history. 1960 Chile earthquake: travel time to Hilo? Compare to actual 15 hours.
  8. User interface. Add dropdown for source location: 5 historical epicenters.

Deliverable

Functional spreadsheet calculator + 2-page validation report against historical events.

Q2

Lines, Slopes & Stoichiometry

Element Babybook

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · D

Math Content

  • Weighted average (atomic mass from isotopes)
  • Percent (isotope abundance)
  • Linear equations (property vs. atomic number)
  • Coordinate plane

Materials

  • Periodic table
  • Isotope abundance data
  • Calculator
  • Art supplies for the book

Step-by-Step

  1. Adopt an element. Pick one with multiple stable isotopes (e.g., carbon, chlorine, copper, magnesium).
  2. Weighted-average atomic mass. Each isotope's mass × abundance. Sum to verify against periodic table.
  3. Isotope percent abundance pie chart. Visualize the isotopes.
  4. Pull data on your element's column. Melting points of all elements in the column.
  5. Linear regression. Plot melting point vs. atomic number. Compute slope (rise/run) and y-intercept. Write the equation y = mx + b.
  6. Predict. Use your linear equation to predict the melting point of an unobserved element (extrapolate or interpolate).
  7. Periodic-table coordinates. Locate your element by (group, period) — its coordinate map.
  8. Write the babybook. 10-page illustrated story incorporating all calculations: birth (formation in stars), family (other elements in its column), traits (linear-equation properties), siblings (isotopes).

Deliverable

Illustrated 10-page "babybook" for the element with all quantitative work integrated.

Catamaran Load Curve Model

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Linear functions (y = mx + b)
  • Systems of equations (intersection of two lines)
  • Slope as rate of change

Materials

  • Catamaran specifications (Hawaiian outrigger or commercial)
  • Graph paper or spreadsheet
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Research catamaran specs. Find empty weight, displacement at full load, hull dimensions.
  2. Build a load curve. Plot draft (depth in water) vs. total weight. It is roughly linear.
  3. Write the linear function. draft = m × weight + b. Compute slope and intercept.
  4. Design a second catamaran. Same hull length but a different beam (width). Predict and plot its load curve.
  5. Solve the system. Where do the two lines intersect? At what weight do both have the same draft?
  6. Apply buoyancy. Find maximum allowable load before draft exceeds safe limit.
  7. Engineering recommendation. 4-page report with both load curves, the system solution, and a justified design choice.

Deliverable

4-page engineering report with graphs.

Q3

Math for Motion (Front-Loaded)

The Apprentices Project — Marble Run Engineering

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • v = d / t (velocity)
  • Linear functions (slope of ramps)
  • Pythagorean theorem (diagonal paths)
  • Projectile range R = v² × sin(2θ) / g

Materials

  • Cardboard, foam, or PVC for tracks
  • Marbles
  • Ruler, protractor
  • Stopwatch
  • Target / landing zone (cup or basket)

Step-by-Step

  1. Define the target. Choose a landing spot ~30 cm from the end of your last ramp.
  2. Design the run. 3–5 ramps with different slopes. Sketch first.
  3. Measure marble velocity. At the end of each ramp, use d / t. Repeat for accuracy.
  4. Compute slopes. Each ramp: slope = rise / run.
  5. Apply Pythagorean theorem. For each diagonal ramp, compute the actual track length given vertical and horizontal distances.
  6. Projectile math. At the end of the run, the marble launches at angle θ with velocity v. Use R = v² × sin(2θ) / g to predict horizontal range.
  7. Build and iterate. Run 10 trials. Track success rate. Adjust angle, ramp slope, or marble launch height.
  8. Engineering log. Record every change you made and its effect.

Deliverable

Physical working marble run + engineering log with all measurements, calculations, and design iterations.

Surf Wave Geometry Photo Analysis

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · C · D

Math Content

  • Pythagorean theorem (wave-face length)
  • Trigonometry (SOH-CAH-TOA)
  • Angles
  • Ratios

Materials

  • 5 photos of Hawaiian surf (own or stock)
  • Ruler
  • Protractor
  • Calculator
  • Known scale reference (surfer height ≈ 1.75 m)

Step-by-Step

  1. Collect 5 surf photos. Pipeline, Waimea, Sunset, Jaws, Honolua. Each must contain a surfer for scale.
  2. Establish scale. Surfer's height = 1.75 m. Measure surfer in photo (cm), find scale factor for that photo.
  3. Measure wave height. Vertical pixels × scale factor = real meters.
  4. Pythagorean wave-face. If wave is h meters tall and slope is s meters horizontally, face length = √(h² + s²).
  5. Measure wave-face angle. Use the protractor on the photo to get θ.
  6. Verify with trig. sin(θ) = h / face_length. Does this match your Pythagorean calculation?
  7. Compare 5 waves. Make a comparison table: height, face length, angle, location.
  8. Analytical write-up. Which wave is steepest? Which is biggest? Connect to surfing difficulty.

Deliverable

5-page analysis with annotated photos, calculations, and comparison table.

Q4

Electricity, Waves & Cosmos

Faraday Project — Hawai‘i Charging Station

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · B · D

Math Content

  • Ohm's Law (V = IR)
  • Linear functions (power vs. current)
  • Inverse variation (frequency × wavelength)
  • Watt-hour budgeting

Materials

  • Small solar panel spec sheet (or hypothetical numbers)
  • Phone-charging requirements (5 V, 2 A typical)
  • Calculator
  • Optional: actual solar panel and breadboard

Step-by-Step

  1. Research solar output. Honolulu averages 5.5 sun-hours per day. A small 10 W panel produces 10 × 5.5 = 55 Wh per day.
  2. Circuit design. Use Ohm's Law: if V = 5 V and R = 2.5 Ω, what is I? Verify your phone charger numbers.
  3. Power-vs.-current linear function. P = V × I. Plot P vs. I for V = 5 V. Slope = 5.
  4. Inverter wavelength. An inverter converts DC to 60 Hz AC. Use f × λ = c (speed of EM waves) to compute wavelength. (Inverse variation: as f goes up, λ goes down.)
  5. Watt-hour budget. Phone battery is 3,000 mAh at 3.7 V = 11.1 Wh per charge. Your station produces 55 Wh/day. How many charges per day?
  6. Cost analysis. Solar panel cost + inverter + cables. Cost per charge over 10-year lifetime. Compare to grid electricity.
  7. Design portfolio. Schematic, calculations, parts list, expected output, payback period.

Deliverable

Detailed design portfolio (8 pages) OR a working prototype with a 4-page documentation.

Orbital Sundial or Pinhole Telescope Build

⏱ 2.5 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Angles (latitude, gnomon angle)
  • Kepler's laws (orbital geometry)
  • Snell's Law (lens refraction)
  • Volume / focal length geometry

Materials

  • For sundial:
  • Cardboard, stick or wire for gnomon
  • Protractor
  • Compass (magnetic)
  • Notebook for observations
  • For telescope:
  • Two convex lenses (one long focal length, one short)
  • Cardboard tube
  • Ruler

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose one project: sundial OR telescope.
  2. (Sundial) Calculate Hawai‘i latitude. ~21°. The gnomon must be tilted at 21° from horizontal.
  3. (Sundial) Build the dial face. Hour lines spaced at 15° (24 hours × 15° = 360°).
  4. (Sundial) Use Kepler's 2nd Law. Earth sweeps equal areas in equal times. This means the shadow does NOT move at uniform angular speed across the day in winter — apply correction.
  5. (Sundial) Calibrate against the actual time. Test for 5 days. Record errors.
  6. (Telescope) Lens equation. 1/f = 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ. Choose lens focal lengths and tube length using Snell's-Law-derived relationship.
  7. (Telescope) Calculate magnification. M = f_objective / f_eyepiece.
  8. (Telescope) Test. Observe the moon. Compare to expected magnification.
  9. Observation log. 5 days of data, with errors and refinements.

Deliverable

Working sundial OR telescope + 5-day observation log + geometry write-up.
