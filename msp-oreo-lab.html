Oreo Cookie Lab

Do Double Stuf Oreos really have twice the crème? Test it.

❓ The Big Question

The name "Double Stuf Oreo" makes a claim. Let's test it.

Essential Question: Do Double Stuf Oreos really have twice as much crème as regular Oreos?

🎯 Objectives

  • Practice the full scientific method on a real product claim.
  • Use a digital scale (and the TARE function) to measure mass in grams.
  • Identify independent, dependent, and control variables.
  • Enter data in a spreadsheet and build a bar graph.
  • Draw a conclusion supported by the data.

🧪 Materials (per lab group)

  • Digital scale (with TARE function)
  • Regular Oreo cookies (~5)
  • Double Stuf Oreo cookies (~5)
  • Paper towels (for a clean work surface)
  • Lab report / data sheet
  • Computer with Google Sheets or Excel
Safety: No safety concerns. If kept clean, the cookies can be eaten at the end!

🔬 Variables & Hypothesis

  • Independent variable: Type of Oreo (regular vs Double Stuf)
  • Dependent variable: Mass of crème filling (grams)
  • Controls: Same brand, same size cookies, same balance, same technique
If type of Oreo is related to mass of crème, then Double Stuf will have ______ the crème of a regular Oreo.

Fill in your prediction — then test it.

🧭 Procedure

Whole cookie mass − Wafer mass = Crème mass

Turn on the digital scale and let it settle to 0.0 g. Place a weigh boat or clean paper towel on the pan, then press TARE to zero it out.
Weigh one whole regular Oreo. Record the mass.
Gently twist off both wafers. Scrape off ALL the crème from each wafer onto the balance pan.
Weigh the crème alone. Record the mass.
Weigh the two clean wafers together. Record the mass. (Check: wafers + crème should ≈ whole cookie.)
Repeat steps 2–5 for the other regular Oreos.
Repeat the whole thing for the Double Stuf Oreos.
Calculate the average crème mass for each type.

📈 Analysis

  1. Enter your data into Google Sheets.
  2. Build a bar graph comparing average regular crème mass vs. average Double Stuf crème mass. (See the Graphing Lab if you need a refresher.)
  3. Calculate the ratio: Double Stuf crème ÷ Regular crème. Is it exactly 2? Bigger? Smaller?
  4. Compare your group's data with the whole class's data. Which do you trust more, and why?

🧠 Conclusion

Write two or three sentences answering:

  1. Did the data support your hypothesis? (Not "was I right" — but "does the evidence match my prediction?")
  2. Do Double Stuf Oreos truly have twice the crème? What ratio did you actually find?
  3. What might cause the difference between your ratio and 2.0? (Measurement error, scraping technique, cookie-to-cookie variation.)
Reflection: If your hypothesis wasn't supported, that's OK. Real scientists learn as much from a "no" as from a "yes."
