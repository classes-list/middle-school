Two project-based summative assessments per quarter — each designed as a 1.5- to 2.5-week capstone tied to the math standards of the quarter and (where possible) directly aligned with a STEM science assessment. Each project lists the math content, materials, step-by-step instructions, and final deliverable. Q1

Lab Math Refresher & Foundations

Hawaiian Watershed Water Budget

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Proportional reasoning
  • Unit conversion (cfs → L/min → m³/s)
  • Percent of a whole
  • Percent error

Materials

  • USGS watershed data for chosen Hawaiian watershed
  • Calculator
  • Watershed map (downloadable)

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose a watershed. Ko‘olau, Wai‘anae, or Waipio (Big Island).
  2. Gather annual numbers. Annual rainfall (m), surface runoff (m³), groundwater recharge (m³), evapotranspiration (m³).
  3. Compute the water budget. Inputs (rainfall) = Outputs (runoff + recharge + ET). Verify the balance.
  4. Compute percentages. What % of rainfall becomes runoff vs. groundwater vs. lost to evaporation?
  5. Convert flow rates. Find a streamflow value in cubic feet per second (cfs) and convert to liters per minute and cubic meters per second.
  6. Compare to USGS published value. Calculate percent error.
  7. Draw the map. Sketch the watershed with arrows showing water flow paths.
  8. Hydrology report. 3-page report: data table, calculations, map, conclusion on watershed health.

Deliverable

3-page hydrology report with watershed map, water-budget table, and conclusion.

Microscopic World Scale Trading Cards

⏱ 1.5 weeks MYP A · D

Math Content

  • Scientific notation with negative exponents
  • Scale ratios between objects
  • Order-of-magnitude comparisons

Materials

  • Cardstock
  • Color printer (or markers)
  • Calculator
  • Reference table of microscopic sizes

Step-by-Step

  1. Pick 12 microscopic objects. Examples: human red blood cell (8 µm), bacterium (1 µm), HIV virus (120 nm), DNA helix (2 nm), mitochondrion (1 µm), ribosome (20 nm), water molecule (0.3 nm).
  2. Convert sizes to scientific notation. Each in meters with negative exponents (e.g., 8 µm = 8 × 10⁻⁶ m).
  3. Choose a card layout. Standard size card with: name, photo or sketch, size in scientific notation, magnification needed to see (= 1 m ÷ size).
  4. Calculate scale ratios. For each card, choose 2 other cards and compute the size ratio. Put as "stat block" on the card (e.g., "100× larger than ribosome").
  5. Rank the deck. Order from largest to smallest.
  6. Print & assemble. 12 cards, double-sided.
  7. Bonus. Add a card showing how many of one object equal the size of a poppy seed (~1 mm).

Deliverable

12-card printed deck + 1-page rank sheet showing the relative-size calculations.

Q2

Brain Data + Tools for Cells

Screen Time vs. Sleep Class Study

⏱ 2 weeks MYP B · C · D

Math Content

  • Survey design / bias controls
  • Sampling
  • Mean, median, range, IQR
  • Percent change between groups

Materials

  • Survey tool (Google Form or paper)
  • Class roster (anonymized)
  • Spreadsheet
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Draft survey questions. "Hours of screen time yesterday", "Hours of sleep last night." Avoid leading questions.
  2. Pilot. Test on 5 classmates; refine wording.
  3. Run for the class. Make it anonymous. Aim for 100% response.
  4. Clean data. Remove implausible responses (e.g., 25 hours of sleep).
  5. Compute statistics. Mean, median, range for screen time and for sleep separately.
  6. Sort into groups. "High screen" (above median) vs. "Low screen" (below median).
  7. Compare sleep across groups. Compute percent change in mean sleep from low-screen to high-screen group.
  8. Write findings. 2-page summary with: methodology, charts, percent change, and caveats (small sample, correlation ≠ causation, self-report bias).

Deliverable

2-page research summary + 1-page survey instrument + data table.

Build-a-Cell with SA:V Calculations

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · C

Math Content

  • Surface area & volume of a sphere
  • SA : V ratio
  • Rearranging formulas (D = m/V)
  • Geometry of organelles

Materials

  • Modeling clay, styrofoam ball, or cardboard for the cell
  • Smaller items for organelles
  • Ruler
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Build a 3D cell. ~30 cm diameter sphere or rounded blob. Include 3 organelles: nucleus, mitochondrion, ribosome (sized roughly to scale).
  2. Whole-cell math. SA = 4πr² and V = (4/3)πr³. Compute both for the cell and the SA:V ratio.
  3. Organelle math. Repeat SA and V calculations for the nucleus (~10 cm diameter) and mitochondrion (~3 cm). Report each SA:V ratio.
  4. Compare ratios. Smaller organelles have higher SA:V — why does that matter biologically?
  5. Rearrange D = m/V. If your model cell has density 1.05 g/cm³ (typical for a cell), use V to compute mass. Then solve for V if mass were known.
  6. Diffusion prediction. Use SA:V to estimate which organelle diffuses oxygen fastest.
  7. Technical report. 2 pages: diagram with measurements, all calculations, biological interpretation.

Deliverable

Physical 3D cell model + 2-page technical report.

Q3

Cells, Geometry & Inequalities

Outbreak Spread Simulation

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · C · D

Math Content

  • Exponential growth (cases = N₀ × R^t)
  • Probability of transmission per contact
  • Inequalities (hospital capacity)
  • Reading log-scale graphs

Materials

  • Spreadsheet
  • Calculator
  • Real outbreak data (COVID 2020, Dengue Hawaii 2015, etc.)

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose an outbreak. Real data, real R₀ (basic reproduction number).
  2. Set parameters. Initial cases N₀, R₀, and infectious period.
  3. Build exponential model. Cases on day t = N₀ × R₀^(t / period). Compute days 1–30 in a spreadsheet.
  4. Calculate probability of transmission. If R₀ = 3 with 10 contacts per day for 5 days, what is per-contact transmission probability?
  5. Apply inequality for hospital capacity. If Hawai‘i has 3,000 hospital beds and 5% of cases need hospitalization, on what day do cases × 5% > 3,000?
  6. Try interventions. Reduce R₀ by 30% (masking + distancing). Recalculate the day capacity is reached.
  7. Graph results. Line graph of cases over time, both with and without intervention. Add a horizontal line for hospital capacity.
  8. Policy memo. 1 page: the math, your recommendation, the cost-benefit reasoning.

Deliverable

Spreadsheet model + 2-page policy memo with graphs.

Marine Protected Area Geometry Design

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · B · D

Math Content

  • Area of polygons
  • Area & circumference of a circle
  • Surface area of reef structures
  • Inequality constraints

Materials

  • Hawaiian coastline map
  • Graph paper or GIS tool
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Choose a coastline. Hanauma Bay, Pūpūkea, Honaunau, Molokini.
  2. Draw the MPA boundary. Polygon (4–6 sides) along the coastline. Mark corners with coordinates.
  3. Compute polygon area. Use the shoelace formula or triangulation.
  4. Add a no-take zone. Circle within the MPA where fishing is fully banned. Compute its area and circumference.
  5. Estimate reef SA. Reefs are 3D. Assume the reef structures occupy 10% of the MPA's bottom area and rise 2 m. Estimate reef surface area.
  6. Apply legal constraints. MPA must be at least 50 acres and at most 200 acres. Convert your area to acres. Does it satisfy?
  7. Write a permit application. Justify size, location, no-take zone, and expected ecological benefit.

Deliverable

Annotated map + 4-page MPA proposal in permit-application format.

Q4

Probability for Genetics

Mock Pedigree Probability Project

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · C

Math Content

  • Punnett squares
  • Compound probability (independent events)
  • Pedigree analysis across 3 generations

Materials

  • Pedigree chart template
  • Three fictional traits (one autosomal dominant, one X-linked recessive, one co-dominant)
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Invent a fictional family. 3 generations. Avoid using real family for sensitivity.
  2. Choose 3 traits. Pick one of each type (autosomal dominant, X-linked recessive, co-dominant). Define alleles.
  3. Assign genotypes. Decide grandparents' genotypes for all 3 traits.
  4. Build Punnett squares. For each pair of parents in each generation. Compute genotype and phenotype probabilities.
  5. Apply compound probability. What is the probability a 3rd-generation child has all 3 dominant phenotypes? Multiply independent probabilities.
  6. Trait pedigree chart. Color-code each generation according to the 3 traits.
  7. Predict the next generation. Given the 3rd generation's expected matings, predict the 4th generation's distribution.

Deliverable

Multi-generation pedigree chart + 2-page probability summary with all Punnett squares.

Hawaiian Monk Seal Population Model

⏱ 2 weeks MYP A · C · D

Math Content

  • Exponential / logistic growth
  • Doubling time (Rule of 70)
  • Proportions across populations
  • Simplified Shannon biodiversity index

Materials

  • NOAA monk seal data
  • Spreadsheet
  • Calculator

Step-by-Step

  1. Research current population. The Hawaiian monk seal is endangered, ~1,500 individuals.
  2. Pull growth-rate data. Annual growth rate from NOAA monitoring (currently ~2% per year).
  3. Apply Rule of 70. Doubling time ≈ 70 / annual growth rate (in %). Compute it.
  4. Project under 3 scenarios. Scenario A (status quo, 2% growth). Scenario B (intensive conservation, 5% growth). Scenario C (climate stressor, 0% growth).
  5. 50-year forecast. Use exponential formula P = P₀ × (1 + r)^t for each scenario.
  6. Biodiversity index. Take 5 species in monk seal habitat with proportions; compute simplified Shannon index (sum p × log₂(p)).
  7. Conservation memo. 1 page on why you recommend a particular scenario.

Deliverable

Spreadsheet model + 1-page conservation memo with graph of 3 projected scenarios.
