Tools of the Trade

How to use every measurement tool in the lab, correctly.

📏 Meter Stick — Distance / Length

Units: millimeters (mm), centimeters (cm), meters (m)

Estimation Tricks (Your Body as a Ruler)

  • Millimeter — the width of a whisker
  • Centimeter — the width of your pinky
  • Meter — nose to the tip of your outstretched arm
You must have a metric ruler. Not an inches-only ruler — a real metric ruler with mm markings.

Reading Practice

When you measure, always read to one decimal place beyond the smallest tick mark. If a ruler is marked in cm and mm, record your answer in cm to the tenths (e.g., 12.3 cm).

⚖️ Digital Scale — Mass

Units: grams (g); switchable to kilograms (kg) or milligrams (mg) on most models.

A digital scale displays mass directly on its LCD.

Important: A digital scale measures MASS, not weight. Mass is how much matter is in an object; it doesn't change with gravity. On the moon, your mass is the same — but you would weigh less.

🎯 The Tare Button — Your Best Friend

The TARE button (sometimes labeled "ZERO") tells the scale to reset the display to 0.0 g, even if there is already something on the pan.

This is how you measure liquids, powders, or anything that needs to sit in a container:

Turn the scale on. Wait for the display to read 0.0 g.
Place your empty container (beaker, weigh boat, cup) on the pan. The display will show the container's mass.
Press TARE. The display resets to 0.0 g — but the container is still there!
Add your sample to the container. The display now shows only the mass of your sample.
Record the reading in grams (with the correct decimal places for your scale's precision).
Why it's brilliant: Without TARE you'd have to weigh the container empty, then full, and subtract. TARE does the subtraction for you, automatically. No math, no rounding error.

Best Practices

  • Place the scale on a flat, level, stable surface — away from breezes or vibrations.
  • Let it warm up for 30 seconds after turning on.
  • Never exceed the maximum capacity (usually printed on the scale). Overloading damages the sensor.
  • Wipe up spills immediately and keep the pan clean between samples.
  • When done, remove the sample, tare back to 0.0, and power off.

Reading Examples

  • Display shows 342.0 g → the sample's mass is 342 g.
  • Display shows 159.2 g after taring → 159.2 g of sample was added.
  • Display shows −3.4 g → something has been removed since the last tare. Investigate!

🧪 Graduated Cylinder — Volume

Units: milliliters (mL)

Line up your eye with the bottom of the meniscus — not above it, not below it.

The Meniscus

Water in a graduated cylinder forms a curved surface called a meniscus — the glass sides pull the water up. Always read the volume at the bottom of the meniscus, with your eye at the same level as that curve.

Why eye level matters: If you look down from above, the meniscus appears higher than it really is and you'll read too much. If you look up from below, it appears lower and you'll read too little. This is called parallax error. Lower your head or raise the cylinder until your eye lines up with the bottom of the curve.

Reading Steps

Place the cylinder on a flat surface.
Bend down so the meniscus is at eye level.
Identify the value of each marked line (2 mL, 1 mL, 0.5 mL, etc.).
Read the volume at the bottom of the curve.

Practice Readings

  • Meniscus at the "24" line → 24 mL
  • Meniscus at the "38" line → 38 mL
  • Small cylinder, meniscus between 2.6 and 2.7 → 2.65 mL

🍶 Beakers & Test Tubes

Beaker

For stirring, mixing, and holding liquids. Not accurate for measuring volume — use a graduated cylinder for that.

Test Tube

Thin glass tube closed at one end. Holds small amounts of material for testing or observation. Not for measuring.

💧 Pipette

A small tube for transferring or delivering measured amounts of liquid.

A one-piece clear plastic transfer pipette. Squeeze the bulb to draw liquid up; release slowly to dispense drop by drop.

How to Use It

  1. Squeeze the rubber bulb before putting the tip in the liquid — never squeeze while the tip is already submerged, or air bubbles will get sucked in.
  2. Dip the tip into the liquid, then slowly release the bulb to draw liquid up into the tube.
  3. Move the pipette to the target container.
  4. Gently squeeze the bulb again to release the liquid, one drop at a time.
  • Each line on a common pipette = 1 mL

⏱️ Timer — Time

Units: seconds (s), minutes (min)

Reading Examples

  • 00:12.85 → 12.85 s
  • 01:06.09 → 1 min, 6.09 s
  • 01:27.97 → 1 min, 27.97 s
  • 00:05.94 → 5.94 s

🧱 Mass Sets

A set of standardized masses (not weights) used to calibrate balances and check other measurements.

Units: grams (g). A 1,000 g mass = 1 kg.

✅ Quick Check

  1. Would you measure the volume of water in a beaker or a graduated cylinder? Why?
  2. What's the difference between mass and weight?
  3. Where on the meniscus do you read the volume?
  4. What must you do to the balance before AND after using it?
