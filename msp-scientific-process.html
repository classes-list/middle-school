The Scientific Process

Question → Hypothesis → Experiment → Data → Analyze → Conclusion.

🧐 Skepticism & Reliable Sources

An old saying: "Believe nothing that you hear and only half of what you see." Scientists don't chase opinions or perspectives — they chase facts, things that can be shown to be true repeatedly.

Have you seen headlines like "New study shows coffee is good for you" on Monday, and "New study shows coffee is bad for you" on Friday? So — does coffee kill you, save you, or both?

The answer lies in your sources. Don't believe the first thing you hear. Always check whether the study is reliable and replicable.

🔍 What Makes a Study Reliable?

  • Testable hypothesis — something you can actually measure
  • Well-designed experiment — procedures clear enough for others to repeat
  • Controlled environment — keep everything the same except one thing
  • Change only ONE variable — otherwise you don't know what caused the result
  • Do multiple trials — one result isn't enough
  • Remove researcher bias — don't "see what you want to see"

🔄 The Scientific Process

Every real experiment follows this six-step loop:

1 · Question

Ask something you're curious about.

2 · Hypothesis

Make an educated guess about the answer.

3 · Experiment

Put your hypothesis to the test.

4 · Collect Data

Record what actually happens.

5 · Analyze

Look at the results with graphs and math.

6 · Conclusion

Was the hypothesis supported? Report your findings.

❓ Step 1: Ask a Testable Question

Your question has to be measurable. Words like better, best, or tastier aren't measurable — they're opinions.

Weak → Strong

Weak (opinion)Strong (testable)
What tastes better, Oreos or Chips Ahoy?Do fingers wrinkle faster in cold or hot water?
Which laundry detergent is better?Which detergent, Tide or All, removes a grass stain with the least amount used?

💡 Step 2: Hypothesis — the "If–Then" Statement

A hypothesis is an educated guess about the answer to your question, written as a prediction:

If [DV] is related to [IV], then [prediction].

Important: If your data proves your hypothesis wrong, don't go back and change your hypothesis. A wrong hypothesis is not a failure — scientists learn just as much from a disproved hypothesis as from a proved one.

🧪 Step 3: Design a Controlled Experiment

A controlled experiment compares two groups:

Control Group

NOT exposed to the change you're testing. This is your baseline.

Experimental Group

IS exposed to the change. Compare this against the control.

Three Types of Variables

  • Independent Variable (IV) — the one you change ("I change"). Goes on the X-axis.
  • Dependent Variable (DV) — the one you measure. Goes on the Y-axis.
  • Controlled Variables — everything else, kept the same.
Golden Rule: Change ONE thing, measure ONE thing.

📊 Step 4 & 5: Collect and Analyze Data

  • Record every measurement carefully — don't skip trials.
  • Organize data in a table before you graph.
  • When graphing: Independent variable on X, Dependent variable on Y.
  • Look for patterns. Calculate error and percent error.

Head to the Graphing Lab to learn how to make clean charts in Google Sheets.

📝 Step 6: Conclusion

State whether the data supports your hypothesis, and describe the relationship between your variables:

"As A increases, B ___________."

Write it so someone else could pick up your report, repeat the experiment, and get the same result.
