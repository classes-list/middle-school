📊 Bar Graph — Compare Categories

Use a bar graph when you want to compare DIFFERENT GROUPS to each other. For example: votes for favorite ice cream flavor, populations of cities, or test scores by class period.
1

Build the table in Google Sheets

A bar graph needs two columns: a CATEGORY column (what you're comparing) and a NUMBER column (the count or measurement).

Include units in the header — "Number of Students" not just "Number."

Ice Cream Survey — Class 4B

💡 Tip: Sort your data from largest to smallest BEFORE making the chart — it makes patterns jump out instantly.
2

Select the data and insert a chart

  1. Click cell A1 and drag down to B6 (selecting ALL the data INCLUDING the headers)
  2. Click Insert menu → Chart
  3. In the Chart editor panel that opens on the right, choose "Column chart" for vertical bars (or "Bar chart" for horizontal)

Google Sheets will guess the chart type. If it picks something else (like a pie chart), change it in the dropdown.

3

Label the axes and add a title

A chart with no labels is useless. In the Chart editor, go to Customize → Chart & axis titles:

  • Chart title: "Favorite Ice Cream Flavors — Class 4B"
  • Horizontal axis title: "Flavor" (the categories on x-axis)
  • Vertical axis title: "Number of Students" (the y-axis count, with units)

The result should look like this:

Favorite Ice Cream Flavors — Class 4B
4

Read the graph correctly

To read a bar graph:

  • Find the bar of interest (e.g., "Chocolate")
  • Look at the TOP of the bar
  • Trace LEFT to the y-axis to read the value

Above: Chocolate was the favorite with 12 votes. Mint was last with 4. You can also see that Chocolate had 3× more votes than Mint (12 ÷ 4).

⚠️ Common mistake: Forgetting units. "Chocolate = 12" means nothing without "12 STUDENTS." Always include units in the axis label.

🥧 Circle Graph (Pie Chart) — Parts of a Whole

Use a circle graph when you want to show how PARTS make up a WHOLE. For example: how you spend a 24-hour day, what fraction of your budget goes to each expense, or types of trash in a landfill.
1

Build the table in Google Sheets

A pie chart needs two columns: a CATEGORY column and a VALUE column. The values must add up to the WHOLE.

How I Spend My Day

1 Activity Hours per Day
2Sleep9
3School7
4Activities3
5Family3
6Free Time2
7TOTAL=SUM(B2:B6)
💡 Tip: Add a TOTAL row using =SUM(B2:B6) to double-check your numbers add to the expected whole (24 hours in a day).
2

Insert the pie chart

  1. Select cells A1:B6 (do NOT include the TOTAL row — it would mess up the chart)
  2. Click Insert → Chart
  3. In the Chart editor, choose "Pie chart" from the dropdown
3

Customize for clarity

In Chart editor → Customize → Pie chart:

  • Slice label: Choose "Percentage" so each slice shows %
  • Slice border: Add a white border so slices are easier to see

In Chart & axis titles:

  • Chart title: "How I Spend My 24-Hour Day"

Your chart should look like this:

How I Spend My 24-Hour Day
📐 Math check: Each slice should be sized based on its FRACTION of the whole. 9 hours of sleep out of 24 hours = 9/24 = 37.5% of the circle. The slice's angle = 360° × (9/24) = 135°.
4

Read the graph correctly

To read a pie chart:

  • Each slice represents a FRACTION of the whole (the whole is always 100%)
  • Bigger slice = bigger fraction
  • Use the LEGEND (the color key) to know which slice means what

From the chart above: I spend the biggest chunk of my day SLEEPING (37.5%), about a third on SCHOOL (29%), and only 8% on FREE TIME.

5

Common Mistakes

⚠️ Too many slices: If you have more than 6–7 categories, the pie becomes unreadable. Group small ones into "Other."
⚠️ Parts don't add to whole: Pie charts only work when categories add to 100%. If kids "vote for multiple flavors," a pie chart is WRONG (use bar instead).
⚠️ No legend: Without a legend, colored slices mean nothing. Always include it.

📈 Scatter Plot (with Trend Line) — Describe Relationships

Use a scatter plot when you want to see HOW TWO THINGS ARE RELATED. For example: hours studied vs. test score, plant height over time, or sleep hours vs. mood rating. A trend line tells you the pattern.
1

Build the table in Google Sheets

A scatter plot needs two numeric columns:

  • The x-axis column — the INDEPENDENT variable (what you control / change)
  • The y-axis column — the DEPENDENT variable (the result you measure)

Include units in each header.

💡 X comes first: Put the INDEPENDENT variable in column A (becomes the x-axis). The DEPENDENT variable goes in column B (becomes the y-axis).
2

Insert a scatter plot

  1. Select cells A1:B9
  2. Click Insert → Chart
  3. In the Chart editor, choose "Scatter chart" from the chart type dropdown

You'll see dots scattered across the graph — one dot per data point.

3

Add a trend line

A scatter plot WITHOUT a trend line just shows dots. The trend line reveals the PATTERN.

  1. In Chart editor → Customize → Series
  2. Scroll down and check the "Trendline" box
  3. Choose type: "Linear" (a straight line through the data)

Don't forget axis titles in Chart & axis titles:

  • Horizontal axis: "Hours Studied"
  • Vertical axis: "Test Score (%)"
  • Chart title: "Study Time vs Test Score"
Study Time vs Test Score
4

Interpret the trend line

The trend line shows the OVERALL pattern. Three possibilities:

📈 POSITIVE

Line goes UP from left to right. As x increases, y increases. Example: more study → higher score.

📉 NEGATIVE

Line goes DOWN from left to right. As x increases, y decreases. Example: more screen time → lower test score.

➡️ NO CORRELATION

Dots scattered randomly, line is roughly flat. No relationship. Example: shoe size vs. IQ.

In our example above, the trend is POSITIVE — every extra hour of studying tends to add ~6 points to the score.

5

Common Mistakes

⚠️ Switching X and Y: Independent variable goes in column A (x-axis). Dependent goes in B (y-axis). Reversing them gives a weird-looking chart.
⚠️ Connecting the dots: Don't connect dots with a zig-zag line — use a TREND LINE instead. The dots are individual observations; the trend line is the pattern.
⚠️ "Correlation = causation": Just because two things trend together doesn't mean one CAUSES the other. Ice cream sales and shark attacks both go up in summer — but ice cream doesn't cause shark attacks!
6
