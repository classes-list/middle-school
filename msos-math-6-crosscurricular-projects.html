🏛️ SOCIAL STUDIES

Pyramid Math — Scaling the Great Pyramid

Math 6 + Social Studies 6, Unit 2 (Ancient Egypt)
⏱ 2 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Ratios & scale factor
  • Pythagorean theorem
  • Surface area of triangular faces
  • Volume of a square pyramid

🏛️ Social Studies Content

  • Ancient Egyptian civilization
  • Pyramid construction & burial beliefs
  • Comparing world cultures (Egyptian ↔ Hawaiian heiau)

📋 Materials

  • Cardstock or thick paper
  • Ruler
  • Protractor
  • Calculator
  • Reference: actual Great Pyramid dimensions (230.4 m base, 146.6 m original height)

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Research. Look up the Great Pyramid of Khufu — base side, original height, number of stones. Express in scientific notation.
  2. Choose a scale. 1:1000 gives a 23 cm base, 14.7 cm tall pyramid — a workable model size.
  3. Compute slant height. Use Pythagorean: slant = √((base/2)² + height²). Show your work for both real and scaled dimensions.
  4. Surface area. Compute the area of one triangular face (½ × base × slant height) × 4 faces.
  5. Volume. V = (1/3) × base² × height. Express in m³ for real and cm³ for model.
  6. Build the model. Draw a net (4 triangles around a square base), cut out, fold and tape.
  7. Compare to Hawaiian heiau. Pu‘ukoholā Heiau is 67 m × 30 m, ~6 m high. Compute volume; how many times smaller than Khufu?
  8. Essay. 2 pages connecting pyramid geometry to Egyptian afterlife beliefs — why did they build it this way?

📦 Deliverable

Physical 1:1000 paper pyramid model + 1-page math worksheet (with all calculations) + 2-page Egyptian-history essay.

📖 ENGLISH LANGUAGE ARTS

Lemonade War Profit Analysis

Math 6 + ELA 6, Unit 2 (The Lemonade War)
⏱ 1.5 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Rates ($ per cup)
  • Percent (profit margin)
  • Linear functions (revenue = price × cups)
  • Break-even analysis

📖 English Language Arts Content

  • Literary analysis of character motivation (Evan vs. Jessie)
  • Connecting fiction to economic reasoning
  • Persuasive writing

📋 Materials

  • A copy of The Lemonade War by Jacqueline Davies
  • Spreadsheet software
  • Calculator

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Re-read. Find every pricing decision in the novel — Evan's $0.50/cup pricing, Jessie's bulk-pricing scheme, ingredient costs.
  2. Build a cost table. For each character: cup price, cost per cup, profit per cup.
  3. Compute profit margin. Profit ÷ revenue × 100% for each strategy.
  4. Project earnings. If each character sold 30 cups per day for 5 days, what would they earn? Build a linear function: revenue = price × cups.
  5. Find the break-even. How many cups must be sold to cover startup cost (lemons, sugar, sign)?
  6. Identify the winner mathematically. Which sibling's strategy was financially smarter?
  7. Connect to character. Why did each sibling choose their strategy? Did Jessie's analytical approach work because she's analytical?
  8. Write the analysis. 2-page literary-economic essay arguing your case with both math and textual evidence.

📦 Deliverable

Spreadsheet model + 2-page literary-economic analysis with citations from the novel.

✝️ RELIGION

Noah's Ark — Volume, Cubits & Capacity

Math 6 + Religion 6, Unit 2 (Old Testament Covenants)
⏱ 1.5 weeks

🧮 Math Content

  • Unit conversion (cubits to meters)
  • Volume of a rectangular prism
  • Ratios & proportions
  • Scale models

✝️ Religion Content

  • Genesis 6–9 narrative
  • Covenant theology (the rainbow promise)
  • Ark as type of Christ / Church
  • Reflection on faith & scale

📋 Materials

  • Bible (Genesis 6:15)
  • Cardboard or cardstock for model
  • Ruler
  • Calculator

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Read Genesis 6:15. Identify the dimensions in cubits: 300 × 50 × 30.
  2. Convert to meters. 1 royal cubit ≈ 0.45 m. Compute: ark is ≈ 135 m × 22.5 m × 13.5 m.
  3. Compute volume. V = l × w × h in cubic meters.
  4. Estimate capacity. Assume small animal needs 1 m³, medium 5 m³, large 30 m³. How many of each could fit?
  5. Compare to modern ships. A modern container ship has volume ≈ 50,000 m³. How many arks would fit inside?
  6. Build a 1:300 scale model. 45 cm × 7.5 cm × 4.5 cm in cardboard.
  7. Reflection. Write 1 page: what does the math reveal about the story? What does it not reveal? Both perspectives matter — ancient narrative used numbers symbolically (300 was a large round number for "huge") and we honor that.

📦 Deliverable

1:300 scale cardboard model + 1-page math worksheet + 1-page theological reflection.

🌏 FOREIGN LANGUAGES

Numbers in Four Languages

Math 6 + Foreign Languages 6 (Hawaiian, French, Spanish, Mandarin)
⏱ 1 week

🧮 Math Content

  • Place value
  • Comparing base systems
  • Pattern recognition in number formation

🌏 Foreign Languages Content

  • Counting 1–100 in 4 languages
  • Cultural number expressions
  • Pronunciation practice
  • Cross-language pattern recognition

📋 Materials

  • Pronunciation references
  • Cardstock for poster
  • Audio resources (Duolingo, etc.)

👣 Step-by-Step

  1. Learn 1–10 in each language. Hawaiian, French, Spanish, Mandarin. Practice pronunciation.
  2. Extend to 11–100. Look for patterns in each language.
  3. Build a comparison chart. For numbers 11, 20, 50, 80, 100, write the spelling and how it's literally constructed.
  4. Mark the math structure. Hawaiian ʻumikūmākahi = "ten and one" (clean). French quatre-vingts = "four twenties" (vigesimal!). Spanish veintiuno = "twenty-one" (compound). Mandarin 二十 = "two ten" (cleanest).
  5. Rank by mathematical cleanness. Which language has the most logical number system? Defend your choice.
  6. Learn ordinal numbers (1st, 2nd, 3rd) in each language.
  7. Build a 4-language number poster. 1–30 in each, with pronunciation marks.
  8. Oral presentation. Count from 1 to 30 in one chosen language for the class.

📦 Deliverable

4-language number poster (1–30) + comparison-and-ranking writeup (1 page) + 2-minute oral presentation.
