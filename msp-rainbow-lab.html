🌈 Rainbow Lab

Mix three primary colors with precise pipette measurements to build all six colors of the rainbow.

🌈 The Big Question

Starting with only red, yellow, and blue colored water, can you use precise volume measurements to create all six colors of the rainbow (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple)?

Essential Question: How carefully do we have to measure to get color mixing right?

🎯 Objectives

  • Accurately measure volumes of liquid using a pipette and graduated cylinder.
  • Practice using metric units (mL).
  • Practice safe laboratory technique — especially avoiding cross-contamination.

⚠️ Safety First

  • Clear your table before you begin.
  • Rinse everything when switching colors — the graduated cylinder and pipette must be clean and empty before touching a new liquid. Cross-contamination will ruin your rainbow.
  • Handle glass test tubes and the graduated cylinder carefully.

🧪 Materials (per lab group)

  • 6 test tubes in a test tube rack
  • 1 × 50 mL graduated cylinder
  • 1 pipette
  • Water cup or sink for rinsing
  • 1 cup with RED liquid
  • 1 cup with YELLOW liquid
  • 1 cup with BLUE liquid
  • Paper towels

🧭 Procedure

Label the six test tubes in order: A, B, C, D, E, F. Between every step, rinse the graduated cylinder and pipette until they are completely empty. Then follow the steps below in order.

Into test tube A, measure and add 10 mL of RED liquid using the graduated cylinder and pipette.
Into test tube C, measure and add 17 mL of YELLOW liquid.
Into test tube E, measure and add 15 mL of BLUE liquid.
From test tube C, use the pipette to measure 4 mL and pour into test tube D.
From test tube E, measure 6 mL and pour into test tube D. Swirl.
From test tube E, measure 2 mL and pour into test tube F.
From test tube A, measure 2 mL and pour into test tube F. Swirl.
From test tube A, measure 4 mL and pour into test tube B.
From test tube C, measure 3 mL and pour into test tube B. Swirl.
Save your results to show your teacher. Once you are checked off, clean your station and complete the Post-Lab worksheet.
Tip: If you skip a rinse, colors will mix in the wrong places and your rainbow will look muddy. Slow down. Rinse. Empty. Then move on.

🌈 Expected Result

If you followed the procedure correctly, your test tube rack should look like this:

Six test tubes A–F showing red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple — volumes 4, 7, 10, 10, 7, 4 mL.

Notice the tube heights mirror across the middle: A and F are the shortest (4 mL), B and E are middle (7 mL), C and D are tallest (10 mL). Symmetry is a good sign that you measured accurately.

📝 Post-Lab Worksheet (17 pts)

Hint: When comparing values (numbers 8 & 9), it's easier if you use KHDUDCM to convert everything to the same unit first.

Metric tools & base units

  1. Circle the tool used to measure mass: meter stick / digital scale / graduated cylinder
  2. What is the base unit for mass? _______________
  3. Circle the tool used to measure length: meter stick / digital scale / graduated cylinder
  4. What is the base unit for length? _______________
  5. Circle the tool used to measure volume: meter stick / digital scale / graduated cylinder
  6. What is the base unit for volume? _______________
  7. What unit of volume is measured using the tool in #5? _______________

Comparing measurements

8. For each letter, circle the value that is larger:

  • a. 10 kg    or    10,000 mg
  • b. 4 mL    or    2 L
  • c. 14 m    or    500 mm

9. For each letter, circle the value that is NOT equal to the others:

  • a. 10 g    0.01 kg    100 cg    10,000 mg
  • b. 400 mm    40 cm    0.004 Hm    0.04 m

Practice with lab tools

10. For each mass below, describe the steps you would take on a digital scale to measure that mass into an empty beaker. Be sure to include when and why you would press the TARE button.

  • a. 430 g of salt
  • b. 26 g of sugar
  • c. 59 g of water

11. Read the volume shown on the graduated cylinders provided by your teacher and record the value.

💭 Discussion

  1. Which two tubes were the hardest to get right? Why do you think so?
  2. What would happen to tube D if you forgot to rinse the pipette between yellow and blue?
  3. Why is the graduated cylinder a better tool for measuring these volumes than a beaker?
  4. Where do you see color mixing like this in the real world? (Printing, painting, stage lighting?)
